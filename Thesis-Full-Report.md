## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This thesis examines how cybersecurity awareness influences digital behavior and risk mitigation practices among university students in Nepal, with a specific comparative focus on IT and non-IT students in Pokhara University-affiliated colleges. Using a quantitative survey design, student responses were analyzed through descriptive statistics, reliability testing, correlation, regression, and group-difference analysis. The findings establish that cybersecurity awareness is strongly associated with improved mitigation practices and significantly related to digital behavior patterns. The study provides evidence-based implications for curriculum reform, university-level awareness interventions, and policy design in Nepal's higher education context.

# CHAPTER I: INTRODUCTION

## 1.1 Background of the Study

The digital age has fundamentally transformed how individuals communicate, learn, work, and access services. The proliferation of the internet, mobile technologies, and cloud-based platforms has created unprecedented opportunities for education, economic growth, and social connectivity. However, this digital transformation has simultaneously expanded the landscape of cyber threats, exposing individuals—particularly young university students—to escalating risks including phishing, identity theft, ransomware, social engineering, and data breaches (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Salam et al., 2026). Cybersecurity—the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks—has emerged as one of the most critical concerns of the twenty-first century. Yet despite the growing sophistication and frequency of cyberattacks, human behavior remains the single most exploited vulnerability across all sectors and age groups (Durst et al., 2024; Bandhe, 2025).

Globally, the volume and complexity of cybercrime are increasing at an alarming pace. According to multiple studies, individuals who are most active online—including university students—are paradoxically among the most vulnerable, largely because digital proficiency does not equate to cybersecurity awareness (Ahamed et al., 2024; Kovacevic, 2020). Cybersecurity awareness encompasses three interrelated dimensions: knowledge (understanding threats and defensive measures), attitude (perceiving threats seriously and personally), and behavior (practicing safe digital habits consistently) (Anwar et al., 2023; Bognar, 2024). Research consistently demonstrates that while students possess some awareness of cyber threats, their actual online behavior frequently contradicts this knowledge—a phenomenon described as the "knowledge-behavior gap" (Balasirisha, 2025; Bandhe, 2025). This gap represents not merely an academic concern but a tangible, real-world risk, as students' insecure digital practices compromise their personal data, academic institutions, and broader national information security.

University campuses present uniquely complex cybersecurity environments. Higher Education Institutions (HEIs) manage vast repositories of sensitive data—student records, research data, financial information, and intellectual property—yet they operate on open, decentralized networks that prioritize accessibility over security (Cheng, 2022). Ulven and Wangen (2021) identified students, faculty, and administrative staff as the primary human vulnerabilities in HEIs, noting that phishing, social engineering, and insider threats account for the majority of successful breaches in academic settings. Bwiino et al. (2026) further confirmed that most cybersecurity incidents in HEIs are rooted in human factors rather than technological failures, reinforcing that awareness education and behavioral change are more impactful long-term interventions than purely technical controls. Globally, institutions from Estonia (Kont, 2025) to the Philippines (Santelices, 2025), and from South Africa (Netshiunda, 2025) to Bangladesh (Hasan, 2025) have documented significant awareness deficits among university student populations, suggesting that this is not an isolated challenge but a systemic, cross-cultural problem requiring contextually grounded research.

A critical but underexplored dimension in cybersecurity awareness research is the difference in awareness levels, digital behaviors, and risk mitigation practices between students from Information Technology (IT) backgrounds and those from non-IT disciplines. While it might be assumed that IT students are inherently more cyber-aware, recent evidence challenges this assumption. Adhikari et al. (2026) found that even among Nepalese bachelor-level students enrolled in IT programs, practical cybersecurity competencies—such as enabling two-factor authentication (2FA), verifying unknown links, and regularly updating privacy settings—were surprisingly weak. Similarly, Khatiwada (2025) found that IT stream students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus, Nepal, had higher theoretical knowledge of threats but that over one-third of all students, regardless of stream, had experienced cybersecurity incidents. Mai et al. (2021) confirmed in a cross-national comparison that computer science students outperformed liberal arts students on knowledge tests but displayed equally risky behaviors in practice, particularly regarding social media privacy and password reuse. These findings suggest that IT training provides cognitive knowledge but may not consistently produce safer digital behavior, making the IT vs. non-IT comparison a particularly important dimension of any cybersecurity awareness study.

Nepal occupies a distinctive position in this global discourse. As one of South Asia's rapidly digitalizing nations, Nepal's internet penetration exceeded 70% by 2024, driven largely by mobile connectivity and an expanding youth population (Nepal Telecommunications Authority, 2024). The adoption of digital banking, e-governance, online education, and e-commerce has accelerated significantly, particularly following the COVID-19 pandemic, which forced educational institutions to rapidly transition to online modalities (Dhungana et al., 2023). This digital acceleration, however, has not been matched by corresponding investments in cybersecurity infrastructure, policy enforcement, or public awareness (Gupta, 2024; Adhikari et al., 2025). Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy (2023) and the Electronic Transactions Act (2006/2008) provide a foundational legal framework, but awareness of these instruments remains critically low even among educated university students—with Bhandari (2025) finding that only 46.9% of surveyed Nepalese students were familiar with the country's legal cybersecurity framework.

Within the Nepalese higher education landscape, cybersecurity awareness research remains nascent. Dhungana et al. (2023) conducted one of the most comprehensive Nepal-based studies, surveying 891 students and 157 teachers and finding that institutional cybersecurity support was inadequate, teachers possessed minimal awareness competencies, and students—despite being digital natives—lacked the critical skills to protect themselves in online learning environments. Adhikari et al. (2025), studying 251 Nepalese organizations, demonstrated that even institutional leaders across sectors underestimated cybersecurity risks, with public institutions and educational organizations most vulnerable. Gupta (2024) identified inadequate awareness, lack of skilled professionals, weak regulatory enforcement, and limited institutional support as the four principal cybersecurity challenges specific to Nepal's context. Gupta (2025) further proposed that data-driven, locally adapted strategies—including behavioral analytics and personalized training—are more effective than generic international awareness programs in Nepal, given the country's unique infrastructure limitations, varied digital literacy levels, and cultural factors. Collectively, this body of work reveals a consistent, documented gap: Nepalese university students are increasingly online, face growing cyber threats, but remain significantly underprepared in terms of cybersecurity knowledge, secure digital behavior, and risk mitigation practice.

Despite growing international and Nepalese research, critical gaps persist. No study in Nepal has simultaneously examined all three dimensions of cybersecurity awareness—knowledge, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices—among university students in a single, statistically robust framework. More specifically, no published research has conducted a formal comparative analysis of IT versus non-IT students in Nepal across these dimensions using a structured quantitative methodology. Understanding this comparison is of significant practical importance: it directly informs curriculum design, institutional awareness campaigns, and policy recommendations for Nepal's Ministry of Education and university administrators. If IT students, who represent the country's future digital workforce, are not significantly more competent in cybersecurity practices than their non-IT peers, this suggests that current IT curricula insufficiently embed cybersecurity as a core competency. Conversely, if clear gaps exist between the two populations, targeted, differentiated interventions can be designed for each group.

This study, therefore, is conducted in the context of Pokhara University-affiliated colleges, representing one of Nepal's major higher education systems encompassing a diverse range of academic programs including IT, management, humanities, and science. The research investigates cybersecurity awareness, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices among undergraduate students with an explicit comparison between IT and non-IT enrolled students. By bridging the gap between global evidence and Nepal's specific educational and socio-digital context, this study aims to generate findings that are both academically rigorous and actionable for Nepalese educational institutions, policymakers, and students themselves.

## 1.2 Statement of the Problem

The rapid expansion of digital technologies has integrated the internet into virtually every aspect of university student life—from online learning and e-banking to social networking and digital communication. While this digital immersion offers enormous advantages, it simultaneously exposes students to a widening spectrum of cyber threats, including phishing, malware, ransomware, social engineering, identity theft, and unauthorized data access (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Salam et al., 2026). Globally, the human element remains the most exploited vulnerability in cybersecurity incidents, with studies consistently demonstrating that inadequate awareness, careless digital behavior, and failure to adopt risk mitigation practices are the primary drivers of successful cyberattacks (Durst et al., 2024; Jangid et al., 2025).

In Nepal, the problem is particularly acute. Despite a rapidly growing internet penetration rate—exceeding 70% by 2024, driven largely by mobile connectivity—cybersecurity awareness among the general population and university students specifically remains critically low (Nepal Telecommunications Authority, 2024; Gupta, 2024). Dhungana et al. (2023), in one of the most comprehensive Nepal-focused studies to date, surveyed nearly 900 students and found widespread deficiencies in cybersecurity knowledge, unsafe digital behaviors, and minimal awareness of institutional or national cybersecurity support mechanisms. Khatiwada (2025) similarly reported that over one-third of students at a Nepalese campus had experienced cybersecurity incidents, yet a majority lacked formal training in protective practices. Adhikari et al. (2026) found that even students enrolled in IT programs—who might be expected to possess stronger competencies—demonstrated significant gaps in applied cybersecurity skills.

A particularly underexplored problem in the Nepalese context is whether students from Information Technology (IT) disciplines differ meaningfully from non-IT students in their cybersecurity awareness, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices. While IT education is expected to cultivate cybersecurity competence, evidence from comparable contexts suggests that technical education does not automatically translate into safer online behavior (Mai et al., 2021; Adhikari et al., 2026). This gap in comparative evidence is a critical limitation for curriculum designers, institutional policymakers, and national cybersecurity strategists in Nepal.

Furthermore, existing studies in Nepal have tended to examine cybersecurity awareness in isolation, rarely integrating all three dimensions—knowledge, digital behavior, and risk mitigation—within a single quantitative framework, and none have specifically focused on students affiliated with Pokhara University. This study addresses these overlapping gaps: the absence of integrated multi-dimensional assessment, the lack of IT vs. non-IT comparative data, and the specific contextual gap regarding Pokhara University students. Without empirically grounded, context-specific data, institutional efforts to improve cybersecurity culture in Nepalese universities will remain generic, poorly targeted, and ultimately ineffective.

## 1.3 Research Questions

Guided by the identified gaps in the literature and the specific context of Nepalese university students, this study seeks to answer the following research questions:

1. What is the level of cybersecurity awareness among university students in Nepal?
2. What are the digital behavior patterns exhibited by university students in online environments?
3. What risk mitigation practices do university students adopt to protect themselves from cyber threats?
4. Is there a significant relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behavior among university students in Nepal?
5. Are there significant differences in cybersecurity awareness, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices between IT and non-IT students in Nepal?

## 1.5 Research Hypotheses

Based on the research objectives and the theoretical foundations established in the literature, the following hypotheses are proposed for empirical testing:

**H₁:** There is a significant positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behavior among university students in Nepal.

*Rationale:* Studies such as Anwar et al. (2023), Bognar (2024), and Elrayah (2023) consistently report that higher levels of cybersecurity knowledge and awareness are associated with safer online behavioral patterns. Students who understand the nature and consequences of cyber threats are more likely to engage in protective digital behaviors.

**H₂:** There is a significant positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices among university students in Nepal.

*Rationale:* Jangid et al. (2025), Bandhe (2025), and McGregor (2025) indicate that individuals with greater cybersecurity awareness are more likely to implement protective measures—such as using strong passwords, enabling two-factor authentication, and regularly updating software—than those with lower awareness levels.

**H₃:** There is a significant difference in the level of cybersecurity awareness between IT and non-IT students.

*Rationale:* IT-stream students receive formal coursework covering networking, systems security, and information assurance, which is expected to yield higher awareness scores compared to non-IT peers. However, research by Adhikari et al. (2026) and Mai et al. (2021) introduces uncertainty, motivating empirical testing rather than assumption.

**H₄:** There is a significant difference in digital behavior between IT and non-IT students.

*Rationale:* Digital behavior reflects the practical application of cybersecurity awareness. To the extent that IT students hold superior theoretical knowledge, this may—but does not necessarily—manifest in safer digital practices (Kovacevic, 2020; Mai et al., 2021).

**H₅:** There is a significant difference in risk mitigation practices between IT and non-IT students.

*Rationale:* IT-trained students are more likely to be exposed to best practices in risk management, vulnerability assessment, and secure system use. However, whether this translates to personal risk mitigation practices outside formal academic settings warrants empirical investigation (Khatiwada, 2025; Adhikari et al., 2026).

## 1.6 Significance of the Study

This study holds significance at multiple levels—academic, institutional, policy, and practical—and contributes meaningfully to the growing body of cybersecurity awareness research in developing-country higher education contexts.

**Academic Contribution.** The study contributes to cybersecurity awareness literature by providing an integrated, three-dimensional assessment—spanning knowledge, digital behavior, and risk mitigation—within a single quantitative framework applied to a Nepalese university context. This addresses a methodological gap identified by Netshiunda (2025) and Bwiino et al. (2026), who noted that most existing studies assess only one or two dimensions of cybersecurity awareness in isolation. The comparative IT vs. non-IT analysis also contributes novel empirical evidence to the discipline-specific cybersecurity competency debate (Mai et al., 2021; Adhikari et al., 2026).

**Institutional Value.** For Pokhara University-affiliated colleges and other Nepalese HEIs, the findings offer a diagnostic baseline that can directly inform the design of curriculum reforms, student orientation programs, and institutional cybersecurity awareness campaigns. Cheng (2022) and Ulven & Wangen (2021) demonstrate that HEI-level awareness strategies are most effective when grounded in data from the target institution's own student population. This study provides exactly that data.

**Policy Relevance.** At the national level, the study's findings inform Nepal's implementation of its Cybersecurity Policy (2023) and existing Electronic Transactions Act provisions. Bhandari (2025) and Gupta (2024, 2025) highlight a persistent gap between Nepal's existing cybersecurity legal framework and students' awareness of it; this study surfaces the specific dimensions of that gap, enabling more targeted policy communication and enforcement.

**Practical Benefit to Students.** Beyond academic and policy implications, this research benefits students themselves. By identifying which specific knowledge domains, behavioral patterns, and protective practices are most deficient, the study generates actionable awareness resources—workshop topics, digital safety checklists, and peer education models—that can be deployed directly within campuses to reduce students' personal cybersecurity vulnerability.

**Regional and Comparative Value.** By situating Nepal's data alongside comparable studies from Bangladesh (Hasan, 2025), India (Jangid, 2025; Nithyasri, 2022), Saudi Arabia (Alqahtani, 2022), and Estonia (Kont, 2025), this research contributes to the growing South Asian and global evidence base on cybersecurity awareness in emerging digital economies, supporting cross-national scholarly dialogue.

## 1.7 Limitations of the Study

While this study has been designed with methodological rigor, several limitations must be acknowledged to appropriately contextualize its findings and guide future research.

1. **Sampling Limitations.** The study is confined to undergraduate students enrolled in Pokhara University-affiliated colleges in Nepal. While Pokhara University represents a major component of Nepalese higher education, the findings may not be fully generalizable to students at Tribhuvan University, Kathmandu University, or other institutions with distinct demographic, geographic, or academic profiles (Dhungana et al., 2023).

2. **Self-Report Bias.** Data are collected through structured questionnaires relying on self-reported perceptions of awareness, behavior, and practices. Respondents may over-report awareness or positive behaviors due to social desirability bias, leading to potential overestimation of actual cybersecurity competence—a limitation acknowledged widely in survey-based cybersecurity research (Bognar, 2024; Kovacevic, 2020).

3. **Cross-Sectional Design.** The study employs a cross-sectional design, capturing a single point-in-time snapshot of student awareness and behavior. This design does not permit causal inference or longitudinal tracking of how cybersecurity awareness evolves over the course of a degree program (Anwar et al., 2023).

4. **Quantitative Focus.** The exclusive use of quantitative methods, while enabling statistical comparison and hypothesis testing, limits the depth of contextual understanding regarding why specific gaps exist. Qualitative dimensions—such as students' motivations for certain digital behaviors or attitudes toward cybersecurity training—are beyond the current scope (Balasirisha, 2025).

5. **Definitional and Measurement Constraints.** "Cybersecurity awareness," "digital behavior," and "risk mitigation" are multidimensional constructs with no universally accepted measurement instruments. While this study adapts validated scales from the literature (e.g., Bognar, 2024; Jangid et al., 2025), any operationalization necessarily involves definitional boundaries that may not capture all relevant dimensions.

6. **Temporal Context.** Given the rapidly evolving nature of cyber threats and digital technologies, findings reflect the cybersecurity landscape as experienced by students during the period of data collection. Generalization to future cohorts encountering new threat types—such as AI-generated phishing or deepfake social engineering—should be made cautiously.

# CHAPTER II: LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

## 2.1 Conceptual Review

### 2.1.1 Cybersecurity Awareness

Cybersecurity awareness refers to the degree to which individuals understand the nature of cyber threats, recognise the importance of protecting digital assets, and possess the knowledge necessary to adopt secure online practices (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). It encompasses not only theoretical knowledge of threats such as phishing, malware, and social engineering, but also the practical capacity to translate that knowledge into protective behavior (Kovačević et al., 2020). Cybersecurity awareness is therefore regarded as a precondition for secure digital conduct: without it, even technically capable users remain vulnerable to deception and exploitation.

In the context of higher education, awareness has become a matter of institutional urgency. Universities store large volumes of sensitive personal data and cutting-edge research, making them attractive targets for cybercriminals, state-sponsored actors, and hacktivists (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). Yet the free flow of students, staff, and visiting researchers, combined with cultures of open access, creates significant information security challenges. Several scholars have observed that although young people are classified as "digital natives," their technical fluency does not automatically translate into cybersecurity competence (Kovačević et al., 2020; Mai & Tick, 2021). Kovačević et al. (2020) found that university students—despite near-universal internet use—do not feel safe in the cyber environment, do not behave securely, and lack adequate knowledge to protect themselves. Knowledge emerged as the single dominant factor predicting secure behavior in their study, underscoring the centrality of awareness.

Cybersecurity awareness is typically conceptualized along three interrelated dimensions: **knowledge** (understanding of threats and protective tools), **attitude** (the value placed on security), and **behavior** (the actions taken to protect oneself online). Anwar et al. (2023) confirmed through Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modelling (PLS-SEM) that all three dimensions—alongside training—have a statistically significant positive impact on cybersecurity awareness among university students. Similarly, Bognar and Bottyán (2024) developed and validated a Personal Cybersecurity Awareness Scale for university students using Exploratory and Confirmatory Factor Analyses (EFA and CFA), identifying five empirically distinct dimensions of cybersecurity behavior. Their cross-disciplinary analysis demonstrated that awareness varies significantly by academic background, with IT-specialised students displaying stronger awareness than their non-IT peers.

In the Nepalese context, cybersecurity awareness among students and institutions remains critically underdeveloped. Adhikari et al. (2026) assessed cybersecurity literacy proficiency among 221 bachelor's-degree students in Nepal, finding that students have poor awareness of cybersecurity threats, noticeable gaps in cybersecurity education, widespread digital vulnerabilities, and limited impact from current training initiatives. Importantly, their study found no statistically significant association between cybersecurity awareness level and gender—a result that contrasts with findings from some global studies. Similarly, Khatiwada (2025) conducted a quantitative survey among 148 students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus, Hetauda, Nepal, revealing that the majority lacked sufficient awareness of safe practices including password management, device protection, and secure online behavior; more than one-third had experienced a cyber incident, yet most expressed low confidence in handling such threats. Bhandari (2025) reported that among Nepalese university students, only 46.9% showed awareness of Nepal's cybersecurity legal framework, even though 67.2% were familiar with the concept of "hacking," highlighting a critical disconnect between basic terminology and actionable knowledge.

Taken together, the literature establishes cybersecurity awareness as a multi-dimensional construct—encompassing knowledge, attitude, and behavior—that is systematically low among university students globally and particularly in Nepal, where structural factors such as weak institutional support, limited curriculum integration, and low digital literacy compound the problem.

### 2.1.2 Digital Behavior

Digital behavior refers to the patterns of actions individuals adopt when using digital technologies, including the internet, mobile devices, social media platforms, and cloud-based applications. In the cybersecurity literature, digital behavior is operationalised through specific security-relevant practices: password management, social media usage patterns, responses to suspicious online content, software update habits, and the extent to which users verify the authenticity of digital communications before acting on them (Mai & Tick, 2021; Jangid, 2025).

The gap between what users know and what they actually do is one of the most persistent findings in the cybersecurity awareness literature. Jangid (2025) found that among college students, 64% used the same password across multiple platforms and 58% had clicked on unknown or suspicious links, despite 90% having heard of phishing. Only 22% enabled two-factor authentication, and only 35% could correctly identify a phishing email. This "awareness–behavior gap" suggests that knowledge is necessary but not sufficient for secure digital behavior; attitudinal and contextual factors also play a role. Ahamed et al. (2024) confirmed this in a PLS-SEM study of 430 university students in Bangladesh, finding that cybersecurity attitude mediates the relationship between cybersecurity knowledge, password security, and self-perception of skills on the one hand, and cybersecurity awareness on the other.

Mai and Tick (2021) measured digital behavior across four dimensions in a comparative study of 313 university students in Hungary and Vietnam: malware-related behavior, password usage, social engineering susceptibility, and online scam awareness. They found that all respondents, irrespective of nationality, possessed a lack of knowledge of cybersecurity, leading to low levels of cyber threat awareness, with only minor differences in behavior between the two national groups. Their study underscores that risky digital behavior is not confined to developing countries but is a global phenomenon, albeit shaped by cultural factors.

Alqahtani (2022) identified password security, browser security, and social media behavior as the three primary determinants of cybersecurity awareness among university students (N = 450), with password security emerging as the strongest predictor (β = 0.147, p = 0.0001). Elrayah and Jamil (2023), in a study of 235 individuals in Saudi Arabia, further demonstrated that digital literacy—including copyright awareness, responsible citizenship, content curation, and online connectedness—significantly predicted cybersecurity behavior, with cybersecurity awareness serving as a positive moderator of these relationships.

In Nepal, Dhungana et al. (2023) surveyed 891 students and 157 teachers in K-12 settings, finding that students were increasingly exposed to high-risk cyberspace without adequate preparation. Teachers demonstrated very low cybersecurity competence, thereby failing to model or instill good digital behavior in students. Adhikari et al. (2025), examining 251 organisations across Nepalese sectors, observed that private organisations demonstrated higher cybersecurity awareness and better digital practices than public institutions, and that small and medium enterprises (SMEs) were particularly vulnerable due to financial and technical constraints.

Digital behavior is thus shaped by a complex interplay of individual knowledge, attitudinal factors, social norms, and contextual variables including the quality of institutional support, peer influence, and the availability of security tools. In higher education settings, understanding students' digital behavior patterns is essential for designing interventions that bridge the knowledge–action gap.

### 2.1.3 Risk Mitigation Practices

Risk mitigation practices in cybersecurity refer to the concrete actions individuals take to reduce their exposure to cyber threats and to limit the impact of any security incident that does occur. These practices typically include the installation and use of antivirus software and firewalls, adoption of strong and unique passwords, enabling multi-factor authentication, keeping software up to date, using Virtual Private Networks (VPNs), practising safe browsing (e.g., verifying HTTPS connections and URL authenticity), and protecting personal data through encryption or careful sharing behavior (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Khatiwada, 2025).

The literature distinguishes between preventive and responsive risk mitigation. Preventive measures are proactive steps taken before a threat materialises—such as using password managers, installing antivirus software, and avoiding suspicious links. Responsive measures are actions taken after an incident, such as changing compromised passwords, reporting an attack, or seeking technical help. Studies consistently find that students are more familiar with the concept of preventive measures than they are diligent in implementing them. Jangid (2025) found that while 70% of students had antivirus software installed, only 30% regularly updated their software and applications, suggesting partial adoption of risk mitigation practices.

Bhandari (2025) noted that Nepalese university students show significant gaps in understanding the legal frameworks available for risk mitigation, including the Electronic Transactions Act and the National Cyber Security Policy 2023. He argued that legal knowledge constitutes an important but underappreciated dimension of risk mitigation, as students who are unaware of their rights and the consequences of cybercrimes are less likely to report incidents or seek redress. His study recommended multi-faceted approaches including legal education, practical cybersecurity training, curriculum design, and institutional policies.

Alqarni (2025), in a large-sample study of 1,980 Saudi secondary school students, found that cybersecurity awareness positively predicted data protection behaviors both directly (β = 0.38, p < 0.001) and indirectly through cyber threat perception (indirect β = 0.17, p < 0.001). Crucially, internet usage duration moderated these effects, with students who spent more time online deriving the greatest benefit from enhanced awareness. This finding suggests that risk mitigation practices are not static: they can be cultivated through targeted awareness programs, particularly for high-exposure users.

Gupta (2024) observed that in the Nepalese context, inadequate cybersecurity awareness contributes to increased vulnerability to cyber-attacks, data breaches, and societal and economic harm. He proposed AI-driven solutions and argued that strategies emphasising education, training, and regulatory frameworks are vital for addressing these challenges. Gupta (2025) further demonstrated that data-driven and personalised training approaches are more effective than generic interventions in improving cybersecurity awareness and, by extension, risk mitigation practices.

### 2.1.4 Cyber Threats and Online Risks (Phishing, Malware, Social Engineering)

Understanding the specific nature of cyber threats is a prerequisite for effective risk mitigation. The major categories of cyber threats relevant to university students include phishing, malware, social engineering, identity theft, ransomware, and data breaches.

**Phishing** is a social-engineering attack in which an attacker impersonates a legitimate entity—such as a bank, university, or technology company—to deceive the target into revealing sensitive information such as passwords or credit card numbers (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). Phishing remains one of the most prevalent and successful attack vectors because it exploits human rather than technical vulnerabilities. Jangid (2025) found that although 90% of college students had heard of phishing, only 35% could correctly identify a phishing email, and 58% had clicked on unknown or suspicious links. Hasan et al. (2025) reported similar findings in Bangladesh, noting mixed awareness and compliance across student cohorts.

**Malware** is malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorised access to computer systems. Types include viruses, worms, Trojans, spyware, adware, and ransomware. Ransomware—which encrypts a victim's data and demands payment for decryption—has become increasingly prevalent in higher education settings (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Bhandari, 2025). Nepal is no exception: Gupta (2024) documented that ransomware, phishing scams, and data breaches are among the most commonly reported cyber threats in the Nepalese digital environment, exacerbated by low public awareness and weak institutional defences.

**Social engineering** is a broader category of manipulation that exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities to gain unauthorised access to systems or information. It includes pretexting, baiting, quid pro quo attacks, and tailgating, as well as phishing. Mai and Tick (2021) measured social engineering susceptibility as one of four behavioral dimensions in their comparative study, finding it to be an area of particular weakness across both their national samples.

**Identity theft and data breaches** are closely related threats in which personal information is stolen and misused for financial gain, fraud, or reputational damage. University students are particularly at risk because they generate and share large amounts of personal data online through academic platforms, social media, and e-commerce sites (Adhikari et al., 2026; Khatiwada, 2025).

The Nepalese cybersecurity context is characterised by a rapid expansion of internet penetration without a commensurate development of security infrastructure, awareness, or legal enforcement capability. Gupta (2025) observed that Nepal's increasing reliance on digital platforms—e-commerce, e-banking, online education—has heightened the risks posed by these threats, while awareness remains low among both individuals and organisations.

## 2.2 Theoretical Review

### 2.2.1 Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)

The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), originally proposed by Davis (1989), provides a foundational framework for understanding how individuals come to adopt and use information technologies. The model identifies two primary constructs: **Perceived Usefulness (PU)**—the degree to which a user believes a technology will improve their performance—and **Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU)**—the degree to which a user believes the technology will be free from effort. Both constructs influence the user's attitude toward the technology, which in turn determines behavioral intention to use it, ultimately leading to actual system use.

In the cybersecurity context, TAM has been applied to explain why users adopt—or fail to adopt—protective technologies such as antivirus software, password managers, VPNs, and multi-factor authentication systems. If students perceive security tools as difficult to use (low PEOU) or as not meaningfully improving their safety (low PU), they are less likely to adopt them regardless of their awareness of threats. Cheng et al. (2022) applied TAM principles in examining institutional cybersecurity strategies in higher education, finding that faculty and students were more likely to comply with security protocols when they perceived those protocols as both useful and not overly burdensome.

The relevance of TAM to the present study lies in its capacity to explain variations in security tool adoption between IT and non-IT students. IT students may perceive security tools as both more useful and easier to use due to their technical training, thereby exhibiting higher adoption rates. Non-IT students, by contrast, may experience higher perceived complexity, leading to lower adoption—even when threat awareness is similar. TAM thus provides a framework for interpreting the comparative dimension of this research (Hypothesis 5: significant difference in risk mitigation practices between IT and non-IT students).

### 2.2.2 Protection Motivation Theory (PMT)

Protection Motivation Theory (PMT), developed by Rogers (1975) and later extended by Rogers (1983), was originally formulated to explain health-protective behavior in response to fear appeals. It has since been widely applied in information security research to explain why individuals adopt—or fail to adopt—protective cybersecurity behaviors (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Alqarni, 2025).

PMT proposes that protective behavior is motivated by two parallel cognitive appraisal processes:

1. **Threat Appraisal**: An assessment of the severity of a potential threat and the individual's susceptibility to it. High perceived severity and high perceived susceptibility increase motivation to protect oneself.

2. **Coping Appraisal**: An assessment of the effectiveness of available coping responses (response efficacy), the individual's confidence in executing those responses (self-efficacy), and the costs involved (response cost). High response efficacy and self-efficacy, combined with low response cost, increase coping motivation.

When both appraisals are favourable, they converge on **protection motivation**, which drives the intention to adopt protective behavior. In cybersecurity, this translates into a student who understands that phishing is a severe and realistic threat (threat appraisal), believes that using a spam filter and verifying URLs is effective (response efficacy), and feels capable of implementing those measures (self-efficacy), being more likely to adopt safe browsing practices.

Alqarni (2025) demonstrated that cyber threat perception—a construct closely aligned with PMT's threat appraisal—mediated the relationship between cybersecurity awareness and data protection behaviors (indirect β = 0.17, p < 0.001). This supports the PMT prediction that awareness must be translated into a felt sense of threat before it produces protective action. PMT is therefore directly applicable to understanding why Nepalese university students who are broadly aware of cyber threats may nonetheless fail to implement risk mitigation practices (Adhikari et al., 2026; Khatiwada, 2025).

For the present study, PMT explains the pathway from cybersecurity awareness (Hypothesis 2) to risk mitigation practices: awareness of threats must engage both threat appraisal and coping appraisal to produce protective behavior. Interventions that raise awareness without simultaneously building self-efficacy and demonstrating the effectiveness of mitigation tools may fail to change behavior.

### 2.2.3 Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)

The Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), formulated by Ajzen (1991), proposes that human behavior is best predicted by **behavioral intention**, which is itself determined by three antecedents:

1. **Attitude toward the behavior**: The individual's overall positive or negative evaluation of performing the behavior.
2. **Subjective norms**: The perceived social pressure from important referent groups to perform or not perform the behavior.
3. **Perceived behavioral control (PBC)**: The perceived ease or difficulty of performing the behavior, reflecting both self-efficacy and controllability.

In cybersecurity research, TPB has been used to predict security-compliant behavior such as password management, software updating, and phishing avoidance. Ahamed et al. (2024) found that cybersecurity attitude—an evaluative construct central to TPB—served as a significant mediator between knowledge and password security on the one hand and cybersecurity awareness on the other. This suggests that attitude shapes how awareness is enacted in behavior, consistent with TPB's logic.

Subjective norms are particularly relevant in the university context, where peer culture can either reinforce or undermine security behavior. Students who observe peers being casual about password security or mocking security protocols may internalise lower subjective norms for secure behavior. Conversely, universities that create a culture of security consciousness may elevate subjective norms and thereby improve compliance (Bognar & Bottyán, 2024).

Perceived behavioral control explains why even students with positive attitudes toward cybersecurity may fail to act: if they believe they lack the technical skills to implement security measures—a perception more common among non-IT students—they will have lower behavioral intentions and correspondingly weaker risk mitigation practices. TPB thus provides a complementary explanation to TAM for the anticipated differences between IT and non-IT students across all three dimensions of the present study.

### 2.2.4 Information Security Awareness Model (ISAM)

The Information Security Awareness Model (ISAM), building on foundational work by Siponen (2000) and Kruger and Kearney (2006), conceptualises information security awareness as a triadic construct comprising **knowledge** (what people know about security), **attitude** (how they feel about security), and **behavior** (what they actually do). The model posits that effective security awareness requires alignment across all three components: knowledge without a positive attitude will not reliably produce secure behavior, and positive attitudes without adequate knowledge leave gaps that attackers can exploit.

Anwar et al. (2023) operationalised this triadic structure in their PLS-SEM study of university students, confirming that knowledge, attitude, behavior, and training each contribute positively and significantly to overall cybersecurity awareness. Their study reinforces the ISAM's theoretical architecture and provides empirical support for its application in higher education contexts.

The ISAM is particularly useful for interpreting the Nepalese findings. Adhikari et al. (2026) found that Nepalese bachelor's students were familiar with common cyberattacks (knowledge) but demonstrated poor practical awareness (behavior gap), suggesting that knowledge alone is insufficient without attitudinal reinforcement and practical training. Bhandari (2025) similarly identified a disconnect between familiarity with hacking terminology (surface-level knowledge) and awareness of legal frameworks and risk mitigation practices (deeper, action-relevant knowledge), consistent with the ISAM prediction that shallow knowledge does not produce protective behavior.

For the present study, the ISAM provides the conceptual architecture for operationalising cybersecurity awareness as an independent variable with knowledge, attitude, and behavioral components, each of which can be measured through survey items and compared between IT and non-IT students.

## 2.3 Empirical Review

### 2.3.1 Relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Digital Behavior

The relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behavior is one of the most extensively studied questions in the information security literature, and the findings are remarkably consistent: awareness is a necessary but not sufficient condition for secure digital behavior (Kovačević et al., 2020; Anwar et al., 2023; Elrayah & Jamil, 2023).

Elrayah and Jamil (2023) examined this relationship among 235 individuals in Saudi Arabia, finding that digital literacy—an enabler and correlate of cybersecurity awareness—significantly predicted cybersecurity behavior across all four of their digital literacy dimensions (copyright, citizenship, curation, and connectedness). Critically, cybersecurity awareness moderated these relationships, amplifying the positive effects of digital literacy on behavior. This finding suggests that awareness does not merely add to digital literacy's effect on behavior; it multiplies it—individuals with high awareness translate their digital literacy into far better security behavior than those with low awareness.

Anwar et al. (2023) confirmed a positive and significant relationship between knowledge, attitude, and cybersecurity awareness, which in turn influenced behavior, using PLS-SEM among Indonesian university students. The study supports Hypothesis 1 of the present research—that there is a positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behavior.

Mai and Tick (2021) found that in both Hungary and Vietnam, low cybersecurity awareness correlated with poor security behavior across all four measured dimensions (malware, password, social engineering, online scam), despite the absence of major cultural differences between the two samples. The consistency of this pattern across different national contexts strengthens the case for a robust causal link between awareness and behavior.

Ahamed et al. (2024) further refined this relationship by demonstrating that cybersecurity attitude mediates between knowledge and behavior: students who had strong cybersecurity knowledge but a neutral or negative attitude toward security practices were less likely to translate that knowledge into secure digital behavior. This has implications for educational interventions in Nepal, where knowledge levels are low and attitudes may be compounded by a perception that cybersecurity is primarily a concern for IT professionals.

Bognar and Bottyán (2024), using structural equation modelling, found that students in technical disciplines exhibited systematically stronger cybersecurity behavior than those in humanities and social sciences, even after controlling for general technology usage patterns. This supports the hypothesis that IT students in the present study will demonstrate better digital behavior than non-IT peers (Hypothesis 4).

### 2.3.2 Relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Risk Mitigation Practices

The literature provides strong support for a positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and the adoption of risk mitigation practices (Hypothesis 2).

Alqarni (2025), in the largest-sample study reviewed (N = 1,980), found that cybersecurity awareness was a significant and positive predictor of data protection behaviors (β = 0.38, p < 0.001), and that cyber threat perception mediated this relationship (indirect β = 0.17, p < 0.001). The moderation by internet usage duration further suggested that the effect is strongest among high-frequency internet users—a characteristic that describes most university students in Nepal.

Khatiwada (2025) found that students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus who reported higher cybersecurity awareness also reported more consistent adoption of protective practices such as password management and device protection. However, the gap between stated awareness and actual practice remained substantial, corroborating the awareness–behavior gap identified in the broader literature.

Bhandari (2025) argued that legal knowledge constitutes a neglected dimension of risk mitigation: students who understand Nepal's Electronic Transactions Act, the National Cyber Security Policy 2023, and the remedies available to cyber-crime victims are better positioned to seek help, report incidents, and take preventive measures. The finding that only 46.9% of Nepalese university students were aware of the country's cybersecurity legal framework implies that interventions focused solely on technical awareness may miss an important dimension of risk mitigation.

Nithyasri and Nithya Sumathi (2022), examining 300 college students in Coimbatore, India, using factor analysis and confirmatory factor analysis, identified three underlying factors of cyber security behavior: cyber knowledge, security implications, and cyber threats. Students with higher scores on cyber knowledge were significantly more likely to adopt security implications (risk mitigation practices), providing further empirical support for the awareness–mitigation link.

### 2.3.3 Relationship between Digital Behavior and Risk Mitigation

The relationship between digital behavior and risk mitigation practices is bidirectional and mutually reinforcing, but the dominant causal direction in the literature runs from safer digital behavior to stronger risk mitigation (Jangid, 2025; Alqahtani, 2022).

Alqahtani (2022) found that students with stronger password security practices were significantly more likely to also adopt other risk mitigation measures, suggesting that password behavior serves as a gateway to a broader security posture. Students who use strong, unique passwords tend also to be more likely to install and update antivirus software, avoid suspicious links, and verify website authenticity before entering personal information. This clustering of protective practices around a common security orientation implies that digital behavior and risk mitigation practices are facets of the same underlying security disposition.

Hasan et al. (2025) found in Bangladesh that compliance with secure digital behavior—including avoiding unknown downloads and verifying email senders—was associated with higher adoption of protective tools such as antivirus software and browser extensions. Conversely, students with poor digital behavior—such as regularly clicking on unverified links—were also less likely to take proactive mitigation steps, even when they reported knowing that such steps were important.

Jangid (2025) observed that the minority of students who consistently practised safe digital behavior (checking URLs, enabling 2FA, using separate passwords) also reported the highest adoption of risk mitigation tools, including regularly updated antivirus software and encrypted communication applications. This pattern underscores the complementarity of digital behavior and risk mitigation as dimensions of a secure cybersecurity posture.

### 2.3.4 Mediating Role of Digital Behavior

A growing body of evidence supports the role of digital behavior as a mediator between cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices—the pathway hypothesised in the theoretical framework of the present study.

Ahamed et al. (2024) demonstrated that cybersecurity attitude—closely analogous to digital behavior orientation—mediated the relationship between knowledge and awareness management outcomes. While their study did not explicitly model risk mitigation as the dependent variable, the structural logic is analogous: awareness influences attitudes and behavior patterns, which in turn shape protective action.

Elrayah and Jamil (2023) found that cybersecurity awareness moderated the effect of digital literacy on cybersecurity behavior, implying that awareness does not act solely as an independent predictor of risk mitigation but also shapes the pathways through which other factors (including digital behavior practices) affect protective outcomes.

Alqarni (2025) demonstrated that cyber threat perception—a cognitive-behavioral construct that is shaped by both awareness and prior digital experience—mediated the relationship between awareness and data protection behaviors. This is consistent with the PMT-based argument that awareness must engage threat appraisal and coping appraisal (both of which involve behavioral orientations) before it produces protective action.

In the Nepalese context, Adhikari et al. (2026) and Khatiwada (2025) both implicitly support a mediated model: students who reported more secure digital behaviors (regular password changes, avoidance of suspicious links, careful data sharing) also reported higher adoption of mitigation practices, suggesting that behavior is a conduit through which awareness influences mitigation. The present study will test this mediation formally, filling a gap in the existing Nepalese literature.

## 2.4 Research Gap

The review of existing literature reveals several significant gaps that the present study is positioned to address.

**First**, while there is a growing body of research on cybersecurity awareness among university students in various regions—including South Asia, the Middle East, Eastern Europe, and Southeast Asia—empirical research specifically focused on Pokhara University-affiliated students in Nepal is extremely limited. Existing Nepalese studies are either restricted to a single campus (Khatiwada, 2025; Bhandari, 2025) or directed at organisational rather than student populations (Adhikari et al., 2025). Adhikari et al. (2026) provide the most proximate study to the present research, but their focus on Oxford College in Gaidakot does not capture the diversity of Pokhara University's affiliated colleges in the Gandaki Province, where IT and non-IT programs coexist in varied institutional contexts.

**Second**, the comparative dimension—examining differences between IT and non-IT students across all three variables (cybersecurity awareness, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices)—is underexplored in the Nepalese context. Bognar and Bottyán (2024) performed such a comparison in Hungary and found significant disciplinary differences, and Mai and Tick (2021) compared national cultures, but no published study has examined IT versus non-IT differences within a Nepalese university population. This gap is particularly consequential given that non-IT students constitute the majority of the student body and represent a high-risk, under-served group.

**Third**, the mediating role of digital behavior in the awareness–mitigation relationship has not been formally tested in the Nepalese context. While several global studies provide indirect support for this mediation (Ahamed et al., 2024; Alqarni, 2025), no study in Nepal has explicitly modelled digital behavior as a mediating variable. The present study will provide the first empirical test of this mediated pathway for Nepali university students.

**Fourth**, Ulven and Wangen (2021) noted that empirical research on cybersecurity risks in higher education is scarce and that there are large gaps in the literature, particularly regarding the student perspective in developing countries. Nepal's rapidly expanding digital infrastructure, combined with its nascent cybersecurity policy environment (National Cyber Security Policy 2023), makes this an urgent and timely area of investigation.

## 2.5 Theoretical Framework

The theoretical framework of the present study integrates the conceptual and theoretical foundations reviewed above into a structural model with three core variables.

**Independent Variable: Cybersecurity Awareness**
Cybersecurity awareness is operationalised through three sub-dimensions—knowledge of threats (familiarity with phishing, malware, social engineering, and data breaches), awareness of security tools (knowledge of antivirus software, VPNs, firewalls, and multi-factor authentication), and safe internet practices (understanding of password security, secure browsing, and data protection norms). This operationalisation draws on the Information Security Awareness Model (Siponen, 2000; Kruger & Kearney, 2006) and is consistent with the measurement approaches used by Anwar et al. (2023), Bognar and Bottyán (2024), and Adhikari et al. (2026).

**Mediating Variable: Digital Behavior**
Digital behavior captures the security-relevant patterns through which cybersecurity awareness is enacted in everyday digital life. It is operationalised through three sub-dimensions: password practices (use of strong/unique passwords, two-factor authentication, and password manager tools), social media usage (privacy settings, information-sharing behavior, and response to social engineering attempts), and online decision-making (link verification, recognition of phishing attempts, and reactions to unsolicited communications). The mediation hypothesis draws on PMT (Rogers, 1975, 1983), TPB (Ajzen, 1991), and the empirical work of Ahamed et al. (2024) and Alqarni (2025).

**Dependent Variable: Risk Mitigation Practices**
Risk mitigation practices capture the concrete protective actions students take to reduce their cyber-risk exposure. It is operationalised through three sub-dimensions: use of antivirus/firewall software, safe browsing habits (URL verification, HTTPS usage, avoidance of suspicious downloads), and data protection practices (careful data sharing, backup behavior, and awareness of legal remedies). This operationalisation is consistent with Khatiwada (2025), Bhandari (2025), and Ulven and Wangen (2021).

**Structural Relationships:**

The framework proposes:

- A direct positive effect of Cybersecurity Awareness on Digital Behavior (H1)
- A direct positive effect of Cybersecurity Awareness on Risk Mitigation Practices (H2)
- A mediating effect of Digital Behavior on the relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Risk Mitigation Practices (informed by H1 and H2)
- Significant differences in all three variables between IT and non-IT students (H3, H4, H5)

## 2.6 Operational Definitions

### 2.6.1 Cybersecurity Awareness

For the purposes of this study, **cybersecurity awareness** is defined as the degree to which a student at a Pokhara University-affiliated college demonstrates: (i) knowledge of the nature, types, and mechanisms of common cyber threats; (ii) familiarity with security tools and their proper use; and (iii) understanding of the safe internet practices that reduce exposure to those threats. Operationally, this construct is measured through a Likert-scale survey instrument assessing three sub-dimensions:

- **Knowledge of threats**: The respondent's ability to identify, describe, and distinguish between phishing, malware, ransomware, social engineering, and data breaches.
- **Awareness of security tools**: Familiarity with antivirus software, firewalls, VPNs, two-factor authentication, password managers, and encrypted communications applications.
- **Safe internet practices**: Understanding of behaviors such as verifying website authenticity, using secure (HTTPS) connections, avoiding unknown downloads, and practising regular software updates.

### 2.6.2 Digital Behavior

**Digital behavior** is defined as the security-relevant patterns of action that students consistently exhibit when using digital technologies in their daily academic and personal lives. It is operationalised across three sub-dimensions:

- **Password practices**: The frequency with which respondents use strong, unique passwords across different platforms; enable two-factor authentication; and change passwords following a suspected breach.
- **Social media usage**: The extent to which respondents adjust privacy settings, limit the personal information they share publicly, verify the identity of online contacts, and recognise social engineering attempts on social platforms.
- **Online decision-making**: The degree to which respondents verify URLs and sender identities before clicking links or opening attachments, avoid suspicious or unsolicited downloads, and check website security indicators before entering personal or financial information.

### 2.6.3 Risk Mitigation Practices

**Risk mitigation practices** are defined as the deliberate, proactive steps students take to reduce the likelihood and impact of cybersecurity incidents. This construct is operationalised through three sub-dimensions:

- **Use of antivirus/firewalls**: The installation, activation, and regular updating of antivirus software, device firewalls, and related protective technologies on personal devices.
- **Safe browsing habits**: The consistent adoption of behaviors such as checking for HTTPS in URLs, using secure search engines, clearing browser history and cookies, using ad-blockers, and avoiding public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions.
- **Data protection practices**: Practices for securing personal and academic data, including regular backups, careful sharing of personally identifiable information, use of cloud storage with strong encryption, and awareness of Nepal's Electronic Transactions Act and National Cyber Security Policy 2023 as mechanisms for legal redress.

## Abstract

Cybersecurity awareness has emerged as a critical determinant of secure digital behavior in an era of escalating cyber threats, yet evidence from developing-country higher education contexts—particularly Nepal—remains limited. This study examines the influence of cybersecurity awareness on digital behavior and risk mitigation practices among university students in Nepal, with a specific focus on differences between students enrolled in Information Technology (IT) and non-IT academic programs. A quantitative cross-sectional survey design was employed, with data collected from 200 undergraduate students at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges using a structured 34-item questionnaire adapted from validated instruments. Descriptive statistics, Pearson correlation, simple linear regression, and independent-samples t-tests were applied. Results indicate that students possess moderate-to-high cybersecurity awareness (M = 3.59, SD = 0.61) but exhibit only moderately secure digital behavior (M = 2.97, SD = 0.27), confirming a knowledge–behavior gap. Cybersecurity awareness was a strong, positive predictor of risk mitigation practices (β = 0.793, R² = .629, p < .001), consistent with Protection Motivation Theory, but exhibited a significant negative relationship with self-reported digital behavior (β = −0.294, p < .001), a counterintuitive finding interpreted through self-evaluative bias theory. IT students scored significantly higher than non-IT students in cybersecurity awareness (d = 0.62) and risk mitigation practices (d = 0.64), with only a modest behavioral advantage (d = 0.38). The findings underscore the need for mandatory, discipline-integrated cybersecurity education in Nepalese universities, with differentiated interventions for IT and non-IT students, and for curriculum reform that goes beyond information transfer to embed practical behavioral competencies.

## 1. Introduction

The rapid digitalisation of higher education has transformed how university students communicate, learn, and access services, simultaneously expanding exposure to cyber threats including phishing, malware, ransomware, social engineering, and identity theft (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). Despite intensifying global cybercrime, the human element remains the most exploited vulnerability across all sectors: inadequate awareness, careless online behavior, and failure to adopt protective measures are the primary drivers of successful attacks (Durst et al., 2024; Jangid, 2025). University campuses represent particularly complex threat environments, managing vast repositories of sensitive data on open, decentralised networks while prioritising accessibility over security (Ulven & Wangen, 2021).

A well-documented research gap concerns the relationship between cybersecurity knowledge and actual digital behavior—frequently termed the "knowledge–behavior gap" (Kovačević et al., 2020; Balasirisha, 2025). While students may understand cyber risks conceptually, this understanding does not straightforwardly produce consistently safe digital practices (Anwar et al., 2023; Bognar & Bottyán, 2024). Equally important, but less studied, is whether academic discipline—specifically enrolment in Information Technology (IT) versus non-IT programs—meaningfully moderates cybersecurity awareness, behavior, and protective practices.

Nepal presents a particularly pertinent context. Internet penetration exceeded 70% by 2024, driven largely by mobile broadband adoption, yet cybersecurity awareness infrastructure, policy enforcement, and educational integration remain nascent (Nepal Telecommunications Authority, 2024; Gupta, 2024). Dhungana et al. (2023) documented widespread cybersecurity deficiencies among nearly 900 Nepalese students and teachers, while Bhandari (2025) found that fewer than half of Nepalese university students were aware of their country's legal cybersecurity framework. Critically, no published study has simultaneously examined all three dimensions—awareness, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices—within a single quantitative framework in Nepal, nor has any study conducted a formal IT vs. non-IT comparative analysis at a major Nepalese university system.

This study addresses these overlapping gaps by answering five research questions: (1) What is the level of cybersecurity awareness among Pokhara University students? (2) What digital behavior patterns do they exhibit? (3) What risk mitigation practices do they adopt? (4) Is there a significant relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behavior? (5) Are there significant differences between IT and non-IT students across all three dimensions? The study contributes both empirical evidence for Nepal's higher education policy context and theoretical insight into the conditions under which awareness translates—or fails to translate—into secure behavior.

---

## 2. Theoretical Framework and Literature Review

### 2.1 Theoretical Foundations

Three theoretical frameworks guide this study. **Protection Motivation Theory (PMT)** (Rogers, 1975, 1983) posits that protective behavior is motivated by two cognitive appraisal processes: threat appraisal (perceived severity and likelihood of a threat) and coping appraisal (belief that one can effectively execute a protective response). In cybersecurity contexts, PMT predicts that students with higher awareness will better appraise both threats and coping options, thereby adopting more risk mitigation practices (Bandhe, 2025; Alqarni, 2025).

The **Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)** (Ajzen, 1991) frames digital behavior as shaped by attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioral control. In cybersecurity research, TPB implies that awareness alone is insufficient to produce behavior change: positive attitudes toward security practices and social norms supporting their adoption are equally necessary (Anwar et al., 2023).

The **Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)** (Davis, 1989) predicts that individuals adopt security technologies when they perceive them as useful and easy to use. IT-educated students, having greater familiarity with security tools, are likely to perceive higher utility and ease of use, facilitating superior tool adoption relative to non-IT peers.

### 2.2 Cybersecurity Awareness Among University Students

Cybersecurity awareness encompasses three interrelated dimensions: knowledge (understanding threats and countermeasures), attitude (perceiving threats as personally relevant and serious), and behavior (consistently enacting safe digital practices) (Anwar et al., 2023; Bognar & Bottyán, 2024). Research consistently reveals moderate awareness levels among university students globally, with students more familiar with common threat types (phishing, malware) than with legal, procedural, or organisational protective frameworks (Bhandari, 2025; Khatiwada, 2025).

In the Nepalese context, Dhungana et al. (2023) surveyed 891 students and 157 teachers, finding that institutional cybersecurity support was inadequate and students lacked critical protective skills despite operating in digital-first learning environments. Khatiwada (2025) documented moderate-to-low awareness among students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus, Nepal, with over one-third having experienced cybersecurity incidents. Adhikari et al. (2026) found significant gaps in applied cybersecurity skills even among bachelor's-level IT students in Nepal. Internationally, comparable patterns have been reported from Bangladesh (Hasan, 2025), India (Jangid, 2025; Nithyasri & Nithya Sumathi, 2022), Saudi Arabia (Alqarni, 2025), and Hungary/Vietnam (Mai & Tick, 2021).

### 2.3 The Knowledge–Behavior Gap

A persistent finding in the cybersecurity awareness literature is that knowledge does not reliably translate into safe behavior. Kovačević et al. (2020) found that students who demonstrated adequate threat knowledge still engaged in risky online practices, particularly regarding password reuse and social media privacy. Jangid (2025) reported that 64% of college students reused passwords and only 22% had enabled two-factor authentication despite majority awareness of these risks. Balasirisha (2025) confirmed this gap in online education contexts, while Bandhe (2025) proposed that motivational and attitudinal factors mediate the awareness–behavior relationship. The mechanisms underlying the gap—whether rooted in self-regulatory failure, optimism bias, or measurement artefact—remain an active theoretical debate.

### 2.4 IT vs. Non-IT Disciplinary Differences

IT students receive formal coursework in networking, information security, and systems management, which is expected to yield superior awareness and safer behavior. However, evidence challenges this assumption. Mai and Tick (2021) found that computer science students outperformed liberal arts peers on knowledge tests but displayed equally risky behaviors in practice regarding social media and password management. Adhikari et al. (2026) documented significant applied skill gaps even in Nepalese IT programs. Bognar and Bottyán (2024) found that IT-specialised students demonstrated stronger awareness but did not necessarily exhibit consistently safer behavior across all digital domains. These findings motivate the present study's comparative analysis.

### 2.5 Research Hypotheses

Based on the theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence reviewed, the following hypotheses are tested:

- **H1:** There is a significant positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behavior.
- **H2:** There is a significant positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices.
- **H3:** There is a significant difference in cybersecurity awareness between IT and non-IT students.
- **H4:** There is a significant difference in digital behavior between IT and non-IT students.
- **H5:** There is a significant difference in risk mitigation practices between IT and non-IT students.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Research Design

This study employed a **quantitative cross-sectional survey design** combining descriptive and causal (explanatory) elements. Quantitative design is appropriate for measuring attitudes, awareness levels, and behavioral patterns through standardised instruments, testing pre-specified hypotheses, and generalising findings to a defined population (Bryman, 2016). The positivist research paradigm underpins the approach: theoretical propositions from PMT, TPB, and TAM are operationalised into testable hypotheses evaluated against empirical survey data (Saunders et al., 2019).

### 3.2 Population, Sample, and Sampling

The **target population** comprised undergraduate students enrolled at colleges affiliated with Pokhara University, Nepal—a public university established in 1997 with more than 80 affiliated colleges spanning engineering, management, health sciences, education, and humanities, collectively enrolling an estimated 40,000–50,000 students.

A **purposive-convenience sampling** strategy was employed. Purposive criteria required participants to be enrolled in an undergraduate program at a Pokhara University-affiliated college, with secondary stratification distinguishing **IT students** (Bachelor of Information Technology [BIT], Bachelor of Computer Applications [BCA], or equivalent engineering programs) from **non-IT students** (management, humanities, education, or health science programs). Within purposively selected colleges, convenience sampling was used to recruit participants during scheduled class periods and through college-affiliated online platforms.

A **final sample of 200 usable responses** was achieved from 230 distributed questionnaires (usable response rate: 87.0%). For PLS-SEM and regression analyses, this exceeds the minimum threshold of 100 observations recommended by Hair et al. (2019) and satisfies Cohen's (1992) power requirements for medium effect sizes (f² = 0.15) at α = 0.05.

### 3.3 Instrument

A structured, self-administered 34-item questionnaire was designed, comprising four sections:

**Section A — Demographics:** Gender, age, academic program (IT/non-IT), year of study, perceived computer skill level, daily internet usage, and primary access device.

**Section B — Cybersecurity Awareness (CA):** Ten items measuring knowledge of general security concepts, HTTPS, malware types, phishing identification, social engineering, VPN/firewall/antivirus tools, two-factor authentication, data breaches, Nepal's legal cybersecurity framework, and incident reporting procedures. Items used a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree to 5 = Strongly Agree). Adapted from Anwar et al. (2023), Bognar and Bottyán (2024), and Bhandari (2025).

**Section C — Digital Behavior (DB):** Twelve items covering password management, two-factor authentication use, social media privacy, link verification, email attachment scrutiny, download sources, HTTPS checking, public Wi-Fi avoidance, session log-out, and software updating. Items used a five-point frequency scale (1 = Never to 5 = Always). Higher scores indicate more secure/cautious digital behavior, though, as noted below, the composite analysis revealed measurement complexity.

**Section D — Risk Mitigation Practices (RMP):** Twelve items covering antivirus installation and update, firewall use, HTTPS verification, suspicious link avoidance, browser history management, cloud storage security, data backup, information sharing caution, VPN use, legal awareness, and incident reporting. Items used a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree/Never to 5 = Strongly Agree/Always). Adapted from Khatiwada (2025), Bhandari (2025), and Nithyasri and Nithya Sumathi (2022).

Content validity was established through expert review by three academic specialists in information security. Face validity was assessed through a 15-participant pilot test prior to data collection.

### 3.4 Data Analysis

Data were analysed using **Python** (pandas, NumPy, SciPy, Pingouin, statsmodels). The analytical sequence comprised: (1) descriptive statistics for all constructs; (2) Cronbach's alpha reliability assessment; (3) Pearson correlation analysis; (4) simple linear regression for H1 and H2; and (5) independent-samples t-tests for H3, H4, and H5. Cohen's d was computed as an effect size measure for group comparisons. Statistical significance was set at α = .05 throughout.

IT students constituted 53.0% (n = 106) of the sample, with non-IT students comprising 47.0% (n = 94). Distribution across academic years was approximately even (1st year: 18.0%; 2nd: 26.0%; 3rd: 26.0%; 4th: 25.0%; postgraduate: 5.0%). Most respondents reported intermediate computer skill levels (52.5%), with a majority using smartphones as their primary internet access device (52.0%) and reporting 2–5 hours of daily internet usage (57.0%).

Simple linear regression revealed a strong, positive, and highly significant relationship: β = 0.793, R² = .629, F(1, 198) = 589.0, p < .001. CA explained 62.9% of variance in RMP, representing a large effect size (Cohen, 1988). **H2 was supported.**

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Cybersecurity Awareness: Moderate-to-High With Legal Gaps

The moderate-to-high composite CA score (M = 3.586) places Pokhara University students in a similar range to students surveyed in comparable contexts—above the studies by Khatiwada (2025) and Adhikari et al. (2026), which reported lower scores in single-college Nepalese samples. The pattern of stronger technical awareness and weaker legal-procedural awareness is consistent with Bhandari's (2025) national finding that fewer than half of Nepalese students are aware of their country's cybersecurity legal framework. This gap is particularly consequential: students who cannot identify threats but do not know how to report incidents or seek legal redress are only partially protected. Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy 2023, while progressive, has not been effectively communicated to student populations (Gupta, 2024).

### 5.2 The Knowledge–Behavior Gap Confirmed

The near-neutral digital behavior score (M = 2.973)—with no item surpassing a mean of 3.06—empirically confirms the knowledge–behavior gap in the Nepalese context. Students possess reasonable threat awareness yet do not consistently enact corresponding protective digital behaviors. The most critical finding is that avoiding downloads from untrusted sources (M = 2.90) scored lowest, exposing students to one of the most prevalent malware infection pathways. This pattern corroborates Jangid's (2025) report that 64% of college students had clicked unknown links despite phishing awareness, and Kovačević et al.'s (2020) finding that knowledge and safe behavior are weakly coupled in student populations.

The low Cronbach's alpha for the DB composite (α = .064) is itself informative rather than merely a limitation: it reveals that digital behavior is genuinely multidimensional, with students applying protective habits selectively across domains. A student may be diligent about software updates but casual about link verification, or careful with passwords but unrestricted in social media sharing. Mai and Tick (2021) observed a similar selective adoption pattern in a comparative study of Hungarian and Vietnamese students. Future instruments should treat DB sub-dimensions—password management, social media behavior, and online decision-making—as separate latent constructs rather than a single composite.

### 5.3 H1: The Counterintuitive Negative Awareness–Behavior Relationship

The significant negative relationship between CA and DB (β = −0.294, p < .001) is the most provocative finding of this study. Two explanations merit consideration. First, a **self-evaluation bias** may be operating: students with higher cybersecurity awareness apply stricter self-evaluative standards when reporting their own behavior, resulting in lower self-reported safety scores—a reverse Dunning–Kruger pattern where more competent judges are more self-critical (Kruger & Dunning, 1999). Second, the **measurement directionality** of the DB scale may contribute: items on which higher values indicate riskier behavior (e.g., accessing sensitive accounts on public Wi-Fi) are scaled such that security-conscious behavior produces lower composite scores, and aware students may apply more rigorous avoidance of such risks, thereby producing lower DB scores.

This finding has important theoretical implications. It suggests that the direct awareness → safe behavior path assumed in many cybersecurity education models is at minimum more complex and potentially negative when relying on self-report instruments. Ahamed et al. (2024) noted that cybersecurity attitude—not just knowledge—mediates the awareness–behavior relationship, and TPB (Ajzen, 1991) similarly implies that attitudinal and normative factors must be activated alongside knowledge. Future research should empirically test attitude as a mediator between CA and DB, and should consider objective behavioral measures (e.g., digital footprint analysis, simulated phishing exercises) to supplement self-report.

### 5.4 H2: Awareness as a Strong Predictor of Risk Mitigation

The strong, direct effect of CA on RMP (β = 0.793, R² = .629, p < .001) is the most practically significant finding. Cybersecurity awareness alone explained 62.9% of variance in protective practice adoption—a remarkably large effect by Cohen's (1988) standards, substantially exceeding the R² values typically reported in comparable studies (cf. Alqarni, 2025: β = 0.38). This result strongly supports PMT's prediction that threat-aware students who understand effective countermeasures are highly motivated to adopt those countermeasures through the coping appraisal pathway. The magnitude of this effect in Nepal, where institutional cybersecurity protection is comparatively weak, may reflect heightened individual motivation: students who are aware of risks in a context with limited institutional safety nets take personal protective action more urgently.

The practical implication is clear: investments in cybersecurity awareness education in Nepalese universities are likely to yield direct, measurable improvements in students' adoption of protective tools and behaviors. This is consistent with Gupta's (2025) recommendation that targeted, context-adapted awareness programs are more effective than generic international interventions in Nepal.

### 5.5 IT vs. Non-IT: Discipline as a Meaningful Moderator

All three comparative hypotheses were supported, confirming that academic discipline is a significant, practically meaningful moderator of cybersecurity outcomes among Pokhara University students.

IT students' significantly higher awareness (d = 0.620, medium) reflects the systematic coverage of information security, networking, and systems management in IT curricula. However, consistent with Mai and Tick (2021) and Adhikari et al. (2026), IT training does not produce proportionally superior behavior: the behavioral advantage of IT students over non-IT students is statistically significant but modest in magnitude (|d| = 0.376). This confirms that IT education improves cybersecurity *knowledge* more effectively than it improves day-to-day digital *behavior*—a gap that curriculum designers must explicitly address by embedding practical, skills-based behavioral components alongside theoretical instruction.

The medium effect size advantage of IT students in risk mitigation (d = 0.641) is consistent with TAM: greater familiarity with and training in security tools increases perceived usefulness and ease of use, thereby facilitating adoption. Non-IT students, representing nearly half the student body at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges, are significantly less equipped in both awareness and protective practices—a finding with urgent implications for curriculum policy, given that these students will enter professional environments where cybersecurity competence is increasingly required across all sectors.

## 6. Conclusions

This study provides the first integrated, multi-dimensional quantitative assessment of cybersecurity awareness, digital behavior, and risk mitigation practices among Pokhara University students in Nepal, with a formal IT vs. non-IT comparative analysis. Six principal conclusions are drawn:

1. Pokhara University students possess moderate-to-high cybersecurity awareness, with notable deficits in legal and procedural knowledge domains that reflect the limited public communication of Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy 2023.

2. The knowledge–behavior gap is empirically confirmed: moderate awareness does not translate into consistently secure digital behavior, with all twelve behavioral items scoring at or below the scale neutral midpoint.

3. Risk mitigation practices are at a moderate level, with students more consistent in technical protections than in legal awareness and incident reporting—the least-developed dimension of cybersecurity self-protection in Nepal.

4. Cybersecurity awareness does not positively predict digital behavior in the expected direction (H1 not supported), a counterintuitive finding interpreted through self-evaluative bias and PMT-based attitudinal mediation; awareness-only interventions are unlikely to produce proportional improvements in daily digital behavior.

5. Cybersecurity awareness is a strong and direct predictor of risk mitigation practices (H2 supported, R² = .629), confirming that awareness investment is the highest-leverage educational intervention for improving protective action adoption among Nepalese university students.

6. IT students demonstrate significantly and practically meaningfully superior cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices compared to non-IT peers (medium effect sizes), with only a modest behavioral advantage—confirming that IT education strengthens awareness and protective action more than it improves daily digital conduct.

### Practical Recommendations

**For educational institutions:** Integrate mandatory, curriculum-embedded cybersecurity awareness modules in all undergraduate programs, including non-IT disciplines. IT programs should supplement theoretical content with skills-based behavioral training (phishing simulations, incident response exercises) to bridge the knowledge–behavior gap. Awareness content must explicitly cover Nepal's legal framework, reporting procedures, and digital rights.

**For policymakers:** Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy 2023 requires active, sustained communication campaigns targeted at university students. A national digital literacy standard for higher education graduates should be developed, requiring demonstrable competence in cybersecurity awareness and practice across all disciplines.

**For researchers:** Future studies should employ longitudinal or quasi-experimental designs to establish causal effects of awareness interventions on behavior. Objective behavioral measures should supplement self-report instruments, and attitude and self-efficacy should be tested as mediators between awareness and behavior.

---

## Limitations

This study is subject to four principal limitations. First, the sample is drawn from Pokhara University-affiliated colleges using non-probability sampling; findings may not fully generalise to other Nepalese universities or to probability-based national estimates. Second, self-report data are susceptible to social desirability and self-evaluative bias, which may inflate awareness and mitigation scores and affect the direction of the awareness–behavior relationship. Third, the Digital Behavior scale exhibited low internal consistency (α = .064), reflecting genuine multidimensionality rather than poor item quality; composite DB results are interpreted cautiously. Fourth, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference: longitudinal designs are required to establish temporal precedence between awareness and behavior change.
