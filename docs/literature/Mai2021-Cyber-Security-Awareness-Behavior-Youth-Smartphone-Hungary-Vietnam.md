## Evidence Mapped to Research Title
_Research title: Cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, and risk mitigation practices among university students in Nepal (Pokhara University)._

- Several scholars have observed that although young people are classified as "digital natives," their technical fluency does not automatically translate into cybersecurity competence (Kovačević et al., 2020; Mai & Tick, 2021).
- In the cybersecurity literature, digital behaviour is operationalised through specific security-relevant practices: password management, social media usage patterns, responses to suspicious online content, software update habits, and the extent to which users verify the authenticity of digital communications before acting on them (Mai & Tick, 2021; Jangid, 2025).
- Mai and Tick (2021) measured digital behaviour across four dimensions in a comparative study of 313 university students in Hungary and Vietnam: malware-related behaviour, password usage, social engineering susceptibility, and online scam awareness.
- Mai and Tick (2021) measured social engineering susceptibility as one of four behavioural dimensions in their comparative study, finding it to be an area of particular weakness across both their national samples.
- Mai and Tick (2021) found that in both Hungary and Vietnam, low cybersecurity awareness correlated with poor security behaviour across all four measured dimensions (malware, password, social engineering, online scam), despite the absence of major cultural differences between the two samples.
- Bognar and Bottyán (2024) performed such a comparison in Hungary and found significant disciplinary differences, and Mai and Tick (2021) compared national cultures, but no published study has examined IT versus non-IT differences within a Nepalese university population.
