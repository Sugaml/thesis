- # CHAPTER II: LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK  ## 2.1 Conceptual Review  ### 2.1.1 Cybersecurity Awareness  Cybersecurity awareness refers to the degree to which individuals understand the nature of cyber threats, recognise the importance of protecting digital assets, and possess the knowledge necessary to adopt secure online practices (Ulven & Wangen, 2021).
- Universities store large volumes of sensitive personal data and cutting-edge research, making them attractive targets for cybercriminals, state-sponsored actors, and hacktivists (Ulven & Wangen, 2021).
- These practices typically include the installation and use of antivirus software and firewalls, adoption of strong and unique passwords, enabling multi-factor authentication, keeping software up to date, using Virtual Private Networks (VPNs), practising safe browsing (e.g., verifying HTTPS connections and URL authenticity), and protecting personal data through encryption or careful sharing behaviour (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Khatiwada, 2025).
- **Phishing** is a social-engineering attack in which an attacker impersonates a legitimate entity—such as a bank, university, or technology company—to deceive the target into revealing sensitive information such as passwords or credit card numbers (Ulven & Wangen, 2021).
- Ransomware—which encrypts a victim's data and demands payment for decryption—has become increasingly prevalent in higher education settings (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Bhandari, 2025).
- It has since been widely applied in information security research to explain why individuals adopt—or fail to adopt—protective cybersecurity behaviours (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Alqarni, 2025).
- **Fourth**, Ulven and Wangen (2021) noted that empirical research on cybersecurity risks in higher education is scarce and that there are large gaps in the literature, particularly regarding the student perspective in developing countries.
- This operationalisation is consistent with Khatiwada (2025), Bhandari (2025), and Ulven and Wangen (2021).
