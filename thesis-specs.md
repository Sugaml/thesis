## 5. Language and Writing Style Rules

### 5.1 General Language
- [ ] Use **clear and concise** writing — avoid jargon and unexplained acronyms.
- [ ] Keep **sentences and paragraphs short**.
- [ ] Delete **unnecessary words and phrases**.
- [ ] Use **active verbs** as much as possible (except Chapter III — see 5.2).
- [ ] Do **not** use emotionally charged language ("very", "extremely") in findings.
- [ ] Be **objective**: do not act as an advocate for any particular position.

### 5.2 Chapter III (Research Methods) — Passive Voice
- Chapter III **must be written in passive voice**:
  - ✓ Correct: "The participants were asked to complete the questionnaire in Appendix 1."
  - ✗ Wrong: "I asked the participants to complete the questionnaire."

### 5.3 Limitations
- Limitations must be discussed transparently so readers can judge confidence in findings.
- Do **not** hide disappointing results; use them to guide recommendations.

## 6. Presenting Data and Results (Chapter IV)

### 6.1 Tables
- Use tables when there is a **considerable amount of information** that would become unwieldy in text.
- Every table must be:
  - Numbered consecutively (Table 1, Table 2 …)
  - Given a descriptive title above the table
  - Cited if sourced from elsewhere
- Tables hold more raw data than charts but require the reader to work the data — use tables for precision, charts for patterns.

### 6.2 Charts and Figures
- Use charts/graphs to show **trends, distributions, and proportions** visually.
- Every figure must be:
  - Numbered consecutively (Figure 1, Figure 2 …)
  - Given a descriptive caption below the figure
  - Cited if sourced from elsewhere
- Ensure figures **add substance** to the text — do not duplicate what the text already says.

### 6.3 Reporting Likert-Scale / Multiple-Choice Data
- Agreement categories may be combined for reporting: "Strongly Agree" + "Agree" = combined agreement percentage.
- State combined percentages clearly in the text, e.g.:
  > "Sixty percent (N = 120) of respondents either strongly agreed or agreed that …"

### 6.4 Reporting Continuous Data
- Report minimum, maximum, and mean/average.
  > "Respondents reported using social media for between 1 and 8 hours per day (M = 3.5 hours)."

### 6.5 Reporting Open-Ended Responses (if any)
- Present either as a **verbatim list** (de-identified) or as **coded themes** (most common to least common).
- Verbatim quotes: remove/replace any identifying words with a general reference in brackets.

### 6.6 Interpreting the Data
- Each reported finding must be followed by an **interpretation** of its meaning, e.g.:
  > *Reporting:* "Only 30% of respondents regularly update their software."  
  > *Interpretation:* "This indicates that the majority of students leave themselves exposed to known vulnerabilities, suggesting a significant gap in risk mitigation practices."

## 7. Chapter-by-Chapter Content Checklist

### Chapter I — Introduction
- [ ] Background / context of the study (cybersecurity in Nepal / University)
- [ ] Problem statement
- [ ] General objective and five specific objectives
- [ ] Research questions or hypotheses (H1–H5)
- [ ] Scope and significance of the study
- [ ] Definition of key terms (cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, risk mitigation)
- [ ] Limitations of the study
- [ ] Outline / organisation of the report

### Chapter II — Literature Review and Theoretical Framework
- [ ] Conceptual review (cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, risk mitigation, cyber threats)
- [ ] Theoretical review (TAM, PMT, TPB — already drafted in Chapter 3; ensure also in Ch2)
- [ ] Empirical review (prior studies — global and Nepal-specific)
- [ ] Research gap / positioning of this study
- [ ] Conceptual / theoretical framework with variable measurement explained
- [ ] All sources cited in APA format

### Chapter III — Research Methods (passive voice throughout)
- [ ] Research design (quantitative, descriptive-comparative, positivist)
- [ ] Target population (University undergraduate students)
- [ ] Sample size determination (Yamane formula; 200 respondents for PLS-SEM)
- [ ] Sampling technique (non-probability: purposive + convenience)
- [ ] Research instrument (questionnaire; construct sources; pilot test if any)
- [ ] Operationalisation / measurement of variables
- [ ] Data collection procedure
- [ ] Data analysis plan (PLS-SEM in SmartPLS 4; t-test/ANOVA in Python with SciPy and Pingouin)
- [ ] Reliability and validity (Cronbach's α, AVE, CR, discriminant validity)
- [ ] Ethical considerations
- [ ] Reference to questionnaire in Appendix

### Chapter IV — Analysis and Results
- [ ] Respondent profile / demographic summary (tables + text)
- [ ] Descriptive statistics for each construct (mean, SD)
- [ ] Reliability and validity results (measurement model)
- [ ] Structural model results (PLS-SEM path coefficients, R², p-values)
- [ ] Hypothesis testing results table (H1–H5)
- [ ] Comparative analysis (t-test / ANOVA: IT vs non-IT) for H3, H4, H5
- [ ] Every table and figure numbered and titled per APA
- [ ] Interpretation follows each result

### Chapter V — Discussion, Conclusion and Implications
- [ ] Discussion of findings (most substantive section — allocated adequate space)
- [ ] Connection of findings back to literature review and theoretical framework
- [ ] Support or rejection of each hypothesis explained
- [ ] Conclusions and summary
- [ ] Practical implications (for students, institutions, policy-makers)
- [ ] Limitations of the study
- [ ] Recommendations for future research
