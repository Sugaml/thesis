## Abstract

Cybersecurity awareness has emerged as a critical determinant of secure digital behaviour in an era of escalating cyber threats, yet evidence from developing-country higher education contexts—particularly Nepal—remains limited. This study examines the influence of cybersecurity awareness on digital behaviour and risk mitigation practices among university students in Nepal, with a specific focus on differences between students enrolled in Information Technology (IT) and non-IT academic programmes. A quantitative cross-sectional survey design was employed, with data collected from 200 undergraduate students at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges using a structured 34-item questionnaire adapted from validated instruments. Descriptive statistics, Pearson correlation, simple linear regression, and independent-samples t-tests were applied. Results indicate that students possess moderate-to-high cybersecurity awareness (M = 3.59, SD = 0.61) but exhibit only moderately secure digital behaviour (M = 2.97, SD = 0.27), confirming a knowledge–behaviour gap. Cybersecurity awareness was a strong, positive predictor of risk mitigation practices (β = 0.793, R² = .629, p < .001), consistent with Protection Motivation Theory, but exhibited a significant negative relationship with self-reported digital behaviour (β = −0.294, p < .001), a counterintuitive finding interpreted through self-evaluative bias theory. IT students scored significantly higher than non-IT students in cybersecurity awareness (d = 0.62) and risk mitigation practices (d = 0.64), with only a modest behavioural advantage (d = 0.38). The findings underscore the need for mandatory, discipline-integrated cybersecurity education in Nepalese universities, with differentiated interventions for IT and non-IT students, and for curriculum reform that goes beyond information transfer to embed practical behavioural competencies.

**Keywords:** cybersecurity awareness; digital behaviour; risk mitigation; university students; Nepal; IT education; knowledge–behaviour gap; Protection Motivation Theory

## 1. Introduction

The rapid digitalisation of higher education has transformed how university students communicate, learn, and access services, simultaneously expanding exposure to cyber threats including phishing, malware, ransomware, social engineering, and identity theft (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). Despite intensifying global cybercrime, the human element remains the most exploited vulnerability across all sectors: inadequate awareness, careless online behaviour, and failure to adopt protective measures are the primary drivers of successful attacks (Durst et al., 2024; Jangid, 2025). University campuses represent particularly complex threat environments, managing vast repositories of sensitive data on open, decentralised networks while prioritising accessibility over security (Ulven & Wangen, 2021).

A well-documented research gap concerns the relationship between cybersecurity knowledge and actual digital behaviour—frequently termed the "knowledge–behaviour gap" (Kovačević et al., 2020; Balasirisha, 2025). While students may understand cyber risks conceptually, this understanding does not straightforwardly produce consistently safe digital practices (Anwar et al., 2023; Bognar & Bottyán, 2024). Equally important, but less studied, is whether academic discipline—specifically enrolment in Information Technology (IT) versus non-IT programmes—meaningfully moderates cybersecurity awareness, behaviour, and protective practices.

Nepal presents a particularly pertinent context. Internet penetration exceeded 70% by 2024, driven largely by mobile broadband adoption, yet cybersecurity awareness infrastructure, policy enforcement, and educational integration remain nascent (Nepal Telecommunications Authority, 2024; Gupta, 2024). Dhungana et al. (2023) documented widespread cybersecurity deficiencies among nearly 900 Nepalese students and teachers, while Bhandari (2025) found that fewer than half of Nepalese university students were aware of their country's legal cybersecurity framework. Critically, no published study has simultaneously examined all three dimensions—awareness, digital behaviour, and risk mitigation practices—within a single quantitative framework in Nepal, nor has any study conducted a formal IT vs. non-IT comparative analysis at a major Nepalese university system.

This study addresses these overlapping gaps by answering five research questions: (1) What is the level of cybersecurity awareness among Pokhara University students? (2) What digital behaviour patterns do they exhibit? (3) What risk mitigation practices do they adopt? (4) Is there a significant relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behaviour? (5) Are there significant differences between IT and non-IT students across all three dimensions? The study contributes both empirical evidence for Nepal's higher education policy context and theoretical insight into the conditions under which awareness translates—or fails to translate—into secure behaviour.

## 2. Theoretical Framework and Literature Review

### 2.1 Theoretical Foundations

Three theoretical frameworks guide this study. **Protection Motivation Theory (PMT)** (Rogers, 1975, 1983) posits that protective behaviour is motivated by two cognitive appraisal processes: threat appraisal (perceived severity and likelihood of a threat) and coping appraisal (belief that one can effectively execute a protective response). In cybersecurity contexts, PMT predicts that students with higher awareness will better appraise both threats and coping options, thereby adopting more risk mitigation practices (Bandhe, 2025; Alqarni, 2025).

The **Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB)** (Ajzen, 1991) frames digital behaviour as shaped by attitudes, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control. In cybersecurity research, TPB implies that awareness alone is insufficient to produce behaviour change: positive attitudes toward security practices and social norms supporting their adoption are equally necessary (Anwar et al., 2023).

The **Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)** (Davis, 1989) predicts that individuals adopt security technologies when they perceive them as useful and easy to use. IT-educated students, having greater familiarity with security tools, are likely to perceive higher utility and ease of use, facilitating superior tool adoption relative to non-IT peers.

### 2.2 Cybersecurity Awareness Among University Students

Cybersecurity awareness encompasses three interrelated dimensions: knowledge (understanding threats and countermeasures), attitude (perceiving threats as personally relevant and serious), and behaviour (consistently enacting safe digital practices) (Anwar et al., 2023; Bognar & Bottyán, 2024). Research consistently reveals moderate awareness levels among university students globally, with students more familiar with common threat types (phishing, malware) than with legal, procedural, or organisational protective frameworks (Bhandari, 2025; Khatiwada, 2025).

In the Nepalese context, Dhungana et al. (2023) surveyed 891 students and 157 teachers, finding that institutional cybersecurity support was inadequate and students lacked critical protective skills despite operating in digital-first learning environments. Khatiwada (2025) documented moderate-to-low awareness among students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus, Nepal, with over one-third having experienced cybersecurity incidents. Adhikari et al. (2026) found significant gaps in applied cybersecurity skills even among bachelor's-level IT students in Nepal. Internationally, comparable patterns have been reported from Bangladesh (Hasan, 2025), India (Jangid, 2025; Nithyasri & Nithya Sumathi, 2022), Saudi Arabia (Alqarni, 2025), and Hungary/Vietnam (Mai & Tick, 2021).

### 2.3 The Knowledge–Behaviour Gap

A persistent finding in the cybersecurity awareness literature is that knowledge does not reliably translate into safe behaviour. Kovačević et al. (2020) found that students who demonstrated adequate threat knowledge still engaged in risky online practices, particularly regarding password reuse and social media privacy. Jangid (2025) reported that 64% of college students reused passwords and only 22% had enabled two-factor authentication despite majority awareness of these risks. Balasirisha (2025) confirmed this gap in online education contexts, while Bandhe (2025) proposed that motivational and attitudinal factors mediate the awareness–behaviour relationship. The mechanisms underlying the gap—whether rooted in self-regulatory failure, optimism bias, or measurement artefact—remain an active theoretical debate.

### 2.4 IT vs. Non-IT Disciplinary Differences

IT students receive formal coursework in networking, information security, and systems management, which is expected to yield superior awareness and safer behaviour. However, evidence challenges this assumption. Mai and Tick (2021) found that computer science students outperformed liberal arts peers on knowledge tests but displayed equally risky behaviours in practice regarding social media and password management. Adhikari et al. (2026) documented significant applied skill gaps even in Nepalese IT programmes. Bognar and Bottyán (2024) found that IT-specialised students demonstrated stronger awareness but did not necessarily exhibit consistently safer behaviour across all digital domains. These findings motivate the present study's comparative analysis.

### 2.5 Research Hypotheses

Based on the theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence reviewed, the following hypotheses are tested:

- **H1:** There is a significant positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behaviour.
- **H2:** There is a significant positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices.
- **H3:** There is a significant difference in cybersecurity awareness between IT and non-IT students.
- **H4:** There is a significant difference in digital behaviour between IT and non-IT students.
- **H5:** There is a significant difference in risk mitigation practices between IT and non-IT students.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Research Design

This study employed a **quantitative cross-sectional survey design** combining descriptive and causal (explanatory) elements. Quantitative design is appropriate for measuring attitudes, awareness levels, and behavioural patterns through standardised instruments, testing pre-specified hypotheses, and generalising findings to a defined population (Bryman, 2016). The positivist research paradigm underpins the approach: theoretical propositions from PMT, TPB, and TAM are operationalised into testable hypotheses evaluated against empirical survey data (Saunders et al., 2019).

### 3.2 Population, Sample, and Sampling

The **target population** comprised undergraduate students enrolled at colleges affiliated with Pokhara University, Nepal—a public university established in 1997 with more than 80 affiliated colleges spanning engineering, management, health sciences, education, and humanities, collectively enrolling an estimated 40,000–50,000 students.

A **purposive-convenience sampling** strategy was employed. Purposive criteria required participants to be enrolled in an undergraduate programme at a Pokhara University-affiliated college, with secondary stratification distinguishing **IT students** (Bachelor of Information Technology [BIT], Bachelor of Computer Applications [BCA], or equivalent engineering programmes) from **non-IT students** (management, humanities, education, or health science programmes). Within purposively selected colleges, convenience sampling was used to recruit participants during scheduled class periods and through college-affiliated online platforms.

A **final sample of 200 usable responses** was achieved from 230 distributed questionnaires (usable response rate: 87.0%). For PLS-SEM and regression analyses, this exceeds the minimum threshold of 100 observations recommended by Hair et al. (2019) and satisfies Cohen's (1992) power requirements for medium effect sizes (f² = 0.15) at α = 0.05.

### 3.3 Instrument

A structured, self-administered 34-item questionnaire was designed, comprising four sections:

**Section A — Demographics:** Gender, age, academic programme (IT/non-IT), year of study, perceived computer skill level, daily internet usage, and primary access device.

**Section B — Cybersecurity Awareness (CA):** Ten items measuring knowledge of general security concepts, HTTPS, malware types, phishing identification, social engineering, VPN/firewall/antivirus tools, two-factor authentication, data breaches, Nepal's legal cybersecurity framework, and incident reporting procedures. Items used a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree to 5 = Strongly Agree). Adapted from Anwar et al. (2023), Bognar and Bottyán (2024), and Bhandari (2025).

**Section C — Digital Behaviour (DB):** Twelve items covering password management, two-factor authentication use, social media privacy, link verification, email attachment scrutiny, download sources, HTTPS checking, public Wi-Fi avoidance, session log-out, and software updating. Items used a five-point frequency scale (1 = Never to 5 = Always). Higher scores indicate more secure/cautious digital behaviour, though, as noted below, the composite analysis revealed measurement complexity.

**Section D — Risk Mitigation Practices (RMP):** Twelve items covering antivirus installation and update, firewall use, HTTPS verification, suspicious link avoidance, browser history management, cloud storage security, data backup, information sharing caution, VPN use, legal awareness, and incident reporting. Items used a five-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree/Never to 5 = Strongly Agree/Always). Adapted from Khatiwada (2025), Bhandari (2025), and Nithyasri and Nithya Sumathi (2022).

Content validity was established through expert review by three academic specialists in information security. Face validity was assessed through a 15-participant pilot test prior to data collection.

### 3.4 Data Analysis

Data were analysed using **Python** (pandas, NumPy, SciPy, Pingouin, statsmodels). The analytical sequence comprised: (1) descriptive statistics for all constructs; (2) Cronbach's alpha reliability assessment; (3) Pearson correlation analysis; (4) simple linear regression for H1 and H2; and (5) independent-samples t-tests for H3, H4, and H5. Cohen's d was computed as an effect size measure for group comparisons. Statistical significance was set at α = .05 throughout.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Participant Characteristics

The sample (N = 200) was predominantly female (51.0%, n = 102), with 41.5% (n = 83) male and 7.5% (n = 15) selecting "other/prefer not to say." The majority (62.5%) were aged 20–25 years. IT students constituted 53.0% (n = 106) of the sample, with non-IT students comprising 47.0% (n = 94). Distribution across academic years was approximately even (1st year: 18.0%; 2nd: 26.0%; 3rd: 26.0%; 4th: 25.0%; postgraduate: 5.0%). Most respondents reported intermediate computer skill levels (52.5%), with a majority using smartphones as their primary internet access device (52.0%) and reporting 2–5 hours of daily internet usage (57.0%).

Students demonstrated **moderate-to-high cybersecurity awareness** (CA: M = 3.586), with stronger scores on general threat knowledge and weaker scores on legal-procedural awareness (Nepal's cybersecurity legislation, M = 3.52; incident reporting knowledge, M = 3.54). **Digital behaviour** scores were near-neutral (DB: M = 2.973), with no item exceeding a mean of 3.06—indicating that secure digital practices are not consistently adopted. The lowest-scoring behaviour was avoiding downloads from untrusted sources (M = 2.90), a common malware delivery vector. **Risk mitigation practices** were moderately positive (RMP: M = 3.456), with HTTPS verification and secure cloud storage use scoring highest, and legal awareness and incident reporting lowest.

### 4.3 Reliability Analysis

Cronbach's alpha coefficients were: CA (α = .820, "Good"), DB (α = .064, below threshold), and RMP (α = .824, "Good"), using Nunnally and Bernstein's (1994) thresholds. The low alpha for the DB scale reflects the multidimensional nature of digital behaviour—twelve items spanning password management, social media privacy, and online decision-making domains exhibit low inter-dimension covariance. Results for the DB composite are therefore interpreted cautiously and supplemented by item-level analysis.

CA correlated strongly and positively with RMP (r = 0.793, p < .001), indicating that more aware students adopt significantly more protective practices. CA correlated significantly but **negatively** with DB (r = −0.294, p < .001). Given that higher DB scores represent more cautious behaviour in this instrument's coding, this negative correlation counterintuitively indicates that more aware students report less safe behaviour—a finding addressed in the Discussion. DB also correlated negatively with RMP (r = −0.245, p < .001), suggesting that poor behavioural hygiene and low protective effort co-occur.

### 4.5 Regression Analysis

**H1: Cybersecurity Awareness → Digital Behaviour**

Simple linear regression revealed a statistically significant but negative relationship: β = −0.294, R² = .086, F(1, 198) = 36.94, p < .001. CA accounted for only 8.6% of variance in DB, and the negative coefficient indicates that higher awareness was associated with lower (more cautious) DB scores rather than the higher positive scores hypothesised. **H1 was not supported in its expected direction.**

**H2: Cybersecurity Awareness → Risk Mitigation Practices**

Simple linear regression revealed a strong, positive, and highly significant relationship: β = 0.793, R² = .629, F(1, 198) = 589.0, p < .001. CA explained 62.9% of variance in RMP, representing a large effect size (Cohen, 1988). **H2 was supported.**

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Cybersecurity Awareness: Moderate-to-High With Legal Gaps

The moderate-to-high composite CA score (M = 3.586) places Pokhara University students in a similar range to students surveyed in comparable contexts—above the studies by Khatiwada (2025) and Adhikari et al. (2026), which reported lower scores in single-college Nepalese samples. The pattern of stronger technical awareness and weaker legal-procedural awareness is consistent with Bhandari's (2025) national finding that fewer than half of Nepalese students are aware of their country's cybersecurity legal framework. This gap is particularly consequential: students who cannot identify threats but do not know how to report incidents or seek legal redress are only partially protected. Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy 2023, while progressive, has not been effectively communicated to student populations (Gupta, 2024).

### 5.2 The Knowledge–Behaviour Gap Confirmed

The near-neutral digital behaviour score (M = 2.973)—with no item surpassing a mean of 3.06—empirically confirms the knowledge–behaviour gap in the Nepalese context. Students possess reasonable threat awareness yet do not consistently enact corresponding protective digital behaviours. The most critical finding is that avoiding downloads from untrusted sources (M = 2.90) scored lowest, exposing students to one of the most prevalent malware infection pathways. This pattern corroborates Jangid's (2025) report that 64% of college students had clicked unknown links despite phishing awareness, and Kovačević et al.'s (2020) finding that knowledge and safe behaviour are weakly coupled in student populations.

The low Cronbach's alpha for the DB composite (α = .064) is itself informative rather than merely a limitation: it reveals that digital behaviour is genuinely multidimensional, with students applying protective habits selectively across domains. A student may be diligent about software updates but casual about link verification, or careful with passwords but unrestricted in social media sharing. Mai and Tick (2021) observed a similar selective adoption pattern in a comparative study of Hungarian and Vietnamese students. Future instruments should treat DB sub-dimensions—password management, social media behaviour, and online decision-making—as separate latent constructs rather than a single composite.

### 5.3 H1: The Counterintuitive Negative Awareness–Behaviour Relationship

The significant negative relationship between CA and DB (β = −0.294, p < .001) is the most provocative finding of this study. Two explanations merit consideration. First, a **self-evaluation bias** may be operating: students with higher cybersecurity awareness apply stricter self-evaluative standards when reporting their own behaviour, resulting in lower self-reported safety scores—a reverse Dunning–Kruger pattern where more competent judges are more self-critical (Kruger & Dunning, 1999). Second, the **measurement directionality** of the DB scale may contribute: items on which higher values indicate riskier behaviour (e.g., accessing sensitive accounts on public Wi-Fi) are scaled such that security-conscious behaviour produces lower composite scores, and aware students may apply more rigorous avoidance of such risks, thereby producing lower DB scores.

This finding has important theoretical implications. It suggests that the direct awareness → safe behaviour path assumed in many cybersecurity education models is at minimum more complex and potentially negative when relying on self-report instruments. Ahamed et al. (2024) noted that cybersecurity attitude—not just knowledge—mediates the awareness–behaviour relationship, and TPB (Ajzen, 1991) similarly implies that attitudinal and normative factors must be activated alongside knowledge. Future research should empirically test attitude as a mediator between CA and DB, and should consider objective behavioural measures (e.g., digital footprint analysis, simulated phishing exercises) to supplement self-report.

### 5.4 H2: Awareness as a Strong Predictor of Risk Mitigation

The strong, direct effect of CA on RMP (β = 0.793, R² = .629, p < .001) is the most practically significant finding. Cybersecurity awareness alone explained 62.9% of variance in protective practice adoption—a remarkably large effect by Cohen's (1988) standards, substantially exceeding the R² values typically reported in comparable studies (cf. Alqarni, 2025: β = 0.38). This result strongly supports PMT's prediction that threat-aware students who understand effective countermeasures are highly motivated to adopt those countermeasures through the coping appraisal pathway. The magnitude of this effect in Nepal, where institutional cybersecurity protection is comparatively weak, may reflect heightened individual motivation: students who are aware of risks in a context with limited institutional safety nets take personal protective action more urgently.

The practical implication is clear: investments in cybersecurity awareness education in Nepalese universities are likely to yield direct, measurable improvements in students' adoption of protective tools and behaviours. This is consistent with Gupta's (2025) recommendation that targeted, context-adapted awareness programmes are more effective than generic international interventions in Nepal.

### 5.5 IT vs. Non-IT: Discipline as a Meaningful Moderator

All three comparative hypotheses were supported, confirming that academic discipline is a significant, practically meaningful moderator of cybersecurity outcomes among Pokhara University students.

IT students' significantly higher awareness (d = 0.620, medium) reflects the systematic coverage of information security, networking, and systems management in IT curricula. However, consistent with Mai and Tick (2021) and Adhikari et al. (2026), IT training does not produce proportionally superior behaviour: the behavioural advantage of IT students over non-IT students is statistically significant but modest in magnitude (|d| = 0.376). This confirms that IT education improves cybersecurity *knowledge* more effectively than it improves day-to-day digital *behaviour*—a gap that curriculum designers must explicitly address by embedding practical, skills-based behavioural components alongside theoretical instruction.

The medium effect size advantage of IT students in risk mitigation (d = 0.641) is consistent with TAM: greater familiarity with and training in security tools increases perceived usefulness and ease of use, thereby facilitating adoption. Non-IT students, representing nearly half the student body at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges, are significantly less equipped in both awareness and protective practices—a finding with urgent implications for curriculum policy, given that these students will enter professional environments where cybersecurity competence is increasingly required across all sectors.

## 6. Conclusions

This study provides the first integrated, multi-dimensional quantitative assessment of cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, and risk mitigation practices among Pokhara University students in Nepal, with a formal IT vs. non-IT comparative analysis. Six principal conclusions are drawn:

1. Pokhara University students possess moderate-to-high cybersecurity awareness, with notable deficits in legal and procedural knowledge domains that reflect the limited public communication of Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy 2023.

2. The knowledge–behaviour gap is empirically confirmed: moderate awareness does not translate into consistently secure digital behaviour, with all twelve behavioural items scoring at or below the scale neutral midpoint.

3. Risk mitigation practices are at a moderate level, with students more consistent in technical protections than in legal awareness and incident reporting—the least-developed dimension of cybersecurity self-protection in Nepal.

4. Cybersecurity awareness does not positively predict digital behaviour in the expected direction (H1 not supported), a counterintuitive finding interpreted through self-evaluative bias and PMT-based attitudinal mediation; awareness-only interventions are unlikely to produce proportional improvements in daily digital behaviour.

5. Cybersecurity awareness is a strong and direct predictor of risk mitigation practices (H2 supported, R² = .629), confirming that awareness investment is the highest-leverage educational intervention for improving protective action adoption among Nepalese university students.

6. IT students demonstrate significantly and practically meaningfully superior cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices compared to non-IT peers (medium effect sizes), with only a modest behavioural advantage—confirming that IT education strengthens awareness and protective action more than it improves daily digital conduct.

### Practical Recommendations

**For educational institutions:** Integrate mandatory, curriculum-embedded cybersecurity awareness modules in all undergraduate programmes, including non-IT disciplines. IT programmes should supplement theoretical content with skills-based behavioural training (phishing simulations, incident response exercises) to bridge the knowledge–behaviour gap. Awareness content must explicitly cover Nepal's legal framework, reporting procedures, and digital rights.

**For policymakers:** Nepal's National Cyber Security Policy 2023 requires active, sustained communication campaigns targeted at university students. A national digital literacy standard for higher education graduates should be developed, requiring demonstrable competence in cybersecurity awareness and practice across all disciplines.

**For researchers:** Future studies should employ longitudinal or quasi-experimental designs to establish causal effects of awareness interventions on behaviour. Objective behavioural measures should supplement self-report instruments, and attitude and self-efficacy should be tested as mediators between awareness and behaviour.

---

## Limitations

This study is subject to four principal limitations. First, the sample is drawn from Pokhara University-affiliated colleges using non-probability sampling; findings may not fully generalise to other Nepalese universities or to probability-based national estimates. Second, self-report data are susceptible to social desirability and self-evaluative bias, which may inflate awareness and mitigation scores and affect the direction of the awareness–behaviour relationship. Third, the Digital Behaviour scale exhibited low internal consistency (α = .064), reflecting genuine multidimensionality rather than poor item quality; composite DB results are interpreted cautiously. Fourth, the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference: longitudinal designs are required to establish temporal precedence between awareness and behaviour change.
