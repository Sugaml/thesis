# CHAPTER V: DISCUSSION, CONCLUSION AND IMPLICATIONS

## 5.1 Introduction

This chapter synthesises the empirical findings reported in Chapter IV, connecting them to the theoretical frameworks and empirical literature reviewed in Chapter II. The discussion addresses each research objective and hypothesis in turn, examines the broader significance of the findings within the Nepalese higher education context, and draws practical and theoretical conclusions. The chapter then outlines the limitations of the study and offers specific recommendations for future research.

---

## 5.2 Discussion of Findings

### 5.2.1 Level of Cybersecurity Awareness Among Pokhara University Students

The study found that Pokhara University students exhibit a moderate-to-high level of cybersecurity awareness, with a composite mean of M = 3.586 (SD = 0.605) on a five-point scale. This finding places the sample in a comparable position to students surveyed in similar contexts: Khatiwada (2025) reported moderate awareness among students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus, Nepal, while Adhikari et al. (2026) documented poor-to-moderate awareness among bachelor's students at an affiliated Nepalese institution. The present study's marginally higher overall mean may reflect the broader, more diverse sample drawn from multiple Pokhara University-affiliated colleges spanning urban and semi-urban settings, as well as the inclusion of IT students who demonstrably elevated the group average.

Students demonstrated relatively stronger awareness of general threat concepts and technical tools (B1: M = 3.65, B4: M = 3.64, B6: M = 3.62) compared to legal and procedural knowledge (B9: M = 3.52, B10: M = 3.54). This pattern is consistent with Bhandari's (2025) finding that Nepalese university students are more likely to know cyber terminology than to understand their legal rights and reporting procedures. The gap between technical awareness and legal-procedural knowledge is particularly significant in the Nepalese context given the relatively recent enactment of the National Cyber Security Policy 2023 and the limited public communication surrounding it (Gupta, 2024). Institutions with a focus purely on technical cybersecurity literacy risk producing students who can identify threats but do not know how to seek redress or report incidents—an incomplete form of awareness with real practical consequences.

From a theoretical perspective, the moderate awareness levels observed are consistent with the Information Security Awareness Model's (ISAM) prediction that knowledge is a necessary precondition for secure behaviour but does not guarantee it. The ISAM, as operationalised by Anwar et al. (2023) and supported by Bognar and Bottyán (2024), posits that effective cybersecurity awareness requires not only knowledge but also positive attitudes and consistent behavioural enactment—dimensions on which this sample showed further room for improvement, as discussed below.

### 5.2.2 Digital Behaviour Patterns

The Digital Behaviour composite score of M = 2.973 (SD = 0.272) indicates that students, on average, engage in digital practices that are only moderately security-conscious. The near-neutral composite mean, combined with the consistently modest individual item means across all twelve behavioural items, paints a picture of students who neither systematically practise secure digital behaviours nor systematically engage in highly risky ones. Rather, the data suggest habitual ambivalence—a pattern in which protective actions (such as enabling two-factor authentication or updating software) are adopted inconsistently and without full commitment.

The lowest-scoring behaviour was avoiding downloads from untrusted sources (C8: M = 2.90), which is among the most exploitable vulnerabilities for malware delivery. The fact that this behaviour scores below the neutral midpoint suggests that a significant proportion of students regularly engage in behaviours that expose their devices to infection risk. This finding corroborates Jangid (2025), who found that 58% of college students had clicked on unknown links despite awareness of phishing, and confirms the persistent knowledge–behaviour gap documented across multiple studies (Kovačević et al., 2020; Balasirisha, 2025).

The low Cronbach's alpha for the DB scale (α = 0.064) warrants specific discussion. This finding indicates that the twelve Digital Behaviour items, while conceptually related, do not co-vary consistently in this sample. One explanation is that digital behaviour is genuinely multidimensional and that different students apply protective habits selectively across domains: a student may be vigilant about password management but careless about link verification, or diligent about software updates but casual about social media privacy settings. This selective adoption of protective behaviours is consistent with Mai and Tick's (2021) finding that students exhibit variable risk profiles across behavioural domains, with no single student being consistently secure across all dimensions. Future studies should consider treating the sub-dimensions (password practices, social media behaviour, online decision-making) as separate latent variables rather than a single composite scale.

### 5.2.3 Risk Mitigation Practices

The Risk Mitigation Practices composite score of M = 3.456 (SD = 0.591) represents a moderate level of protective action—marginally higher than the Digital Behaviour score, suggesting that students are somewhat more consistent in adopting risk mitigation tools (antivirus software, secure browsing) than in practising moment-to-moment secure digital behaviour. The strongest item was checking for HTTPS before entering personal data (D4: M = 3.54), reflecting basic secure browsing awareness. The weakest items were awareness of legal remedies (D11: M = 3.37) and reporting of cybersecurity incidents (D12: M = 3.42), reinforcing the legal-awareness deficit identified in both the awareness and behaviour dimensions.

The moderate level of risk mitigation adoption found in this study is consistent with findings from South Asia and beyond. Nithyasri and Nithya Sumathi (2022) reported partial adoption of risk mitigation practices among Indian college students, noting that antivirus installation was common but regular software updates were not. Alqarni (2025) found that cybersecurity awareness was the strongest predictor of data protection behaviours (β = 0.38, p < .001), which aligns with the current study's finding of a strong direct effect of CA on RMP (β = 0.774, R² = .629). The magnitude of this effect in the present study is substantially larger, suggesting that among Nepalese university students, the connection between awareness and protective practices may be particularly direct and strong—possibly because those who are aware of risks in Nepal's cyber environment, where institutional protection is weaker, are motivated to take individual protective actions.

### 5.2.4 Hypothesis H1: Relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Digital Behaviour

Hypothesis H1, which predicted a positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behaviour, was not supported in its expected direction. The regression analysis revealed a statistically significant but negative relationship (β = −0.294, R² = .086, p < .001), indicating that students with higher cybersecurity awareness actually scored lower on the Digital Behaviour composite. This counterintuitive finding merits careful interpretation.

As noted in Chapter IV, the negative direction can be interpreted in two ways. First, it may reflect a self-evaluation bias: students with higher awareness apply stricter standards when assessing their own behaviour, perceiving themselves as less secure than their lower-awareness peers who lack the knowledge to recognise deficiencies in their own practices. This is consistent with the Dunning–Kruger effect in reverse: more knowledgeable individuals tend to be more critical of their own performance (Kruger & Dunning, 1999). Second, it is possible that the Digital Behaviour scale, with items scored such that higher values indicate riskier behaviour (i.e., not reversed to a protective polarity), reverses the expected direction when correlated with awareness.

The negative correlation between CA and DB has been reported by some other studies. Bandhe (2025) noted that individuals with higher awareness are sometimes found to be more self-critical when self-reporting behaviours, and Ahamed et al. (2024) found that cybersecurity attitude—not just knowledge—was a mediating factor between awareness and behaviour, suggesting that awareness alone does not straightforwardly produce secure behaviour. In the broader literature, Kovačević et al. (2020) found that students do not feel safe in the cyber environment despite varying awareness levels, suggesting a complex and non-linear relationship.

The failure to confirm H1 in the expected direction is a significant finding that contributes to the cybersecurity awareness literature. It suggests that cybersecurity education programmes that focus solely on raising knowledge may not automatically produce proportionally safer digital behaviour, and that attitudinal and motivational factors—as articulated by the Theory of Planned Behaviour (Ajzen, 1991)—must also be addressed. Future research should explore whether the introduction of practical skills training and peer accountability mechanisms, rather than information transfer alone, produces stronger and more direct improvements in daily digital behaviour.

### 5.2.5 Hypothesis H2: Relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Risk Mitigation Practices

Hypothesis H2, which predicted a positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices, was strongly supported (β = 0.793, R² = .629, p < .001). Cybersecurity awareness explained 62.9% of the variance in risk mitigation practices—a large and practically meaningful effect. This finding indicates that among Pokhara University students, the most important driver of protective action is awareness itself: students who understand threats, recognise security tools, and know safe internet practices are substantially more likely to install antivirus software, use HTTPS, back up files, and adopt other risk mitigation measures.

This result is theoretically coherent with Protection Motivation Theory (Rogers, 1975, 1983), which proposes that protective behaviour is driven by both threat appraisal (recognising that threats are real and severe) and coping appraisal (believing that specific actions can reduce risk). Students with higher cybersecurity awareness are better equipped to engage both appraisal processes: they recognise the severity and likelihood of threats (threat appraisal) and are familiar with effective protective tools (coping appraisal). The very high R² value suggests that in the Nepalese university context, this PMT-based pathway operates with particular strength—the majority of variance in protective practice adoption is explained by awareness alone.

The finding has important practical implications. It suggests that investments in cybersecurity awareness education are likely to translate directly into improved adoption of protective practices among Nepalese students. This is consistent with Gupta's (2025) recommendation that data-driven, targeted awareness programmes are more effective than generic interventions in Nepal's digital landscape, and with Dhungana et al.'s (2023) finding that where institutional awareness support is provided, student protective behaviour improves.

### 5.2.6 IT vs. Non-IT Comparison (H3, H4, H5)

All three comparative hypotheses (H3, H4, H5) were supported, confirming that academic discipline is a significant moderating factor in cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, and risk mitigation practices among Pokhara University students.

**H3 — Cybersecurity Awareness.** IT students (M = 3.754) scored significantly and meaningfully higher in cybersecurity awareness than non-IT students (M = 3.397), with a medium effect size (d = 0.620). This finding is consistent with Bognar and Bottyán (2024), who reported that IT-specialised students displayed systematically stronger cybersecurity awareness than humanities and social science students. It also aligns with Adhikari et al. (2026), who found that even among Nepalese IT students, awareness was deficient—suggesting that while IT training confers an advantage, it does not produce optimal awareness. The practical implication is that targeted, supplementary cybersecurity education is needed for non-IT students, who represent nearly half the student body at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges.

**H4 — Digital Behaviour.** The finding that IT students (M = 2.926) scored lower on the Digital Behaviour composite than non-IT students (M = 3.027) is consistent with the interpretation that IT students, with higher awareness, are more self-critical when assessing their own behaviours. More importantly, this result—while statistically significant (p = .009)—has a relatively small-to-medium effect size (|d| = 0.376), suggesting that the practical behavioural advantage of IT training is modest. This is consistent with Mai and Tick's (2021) cross-national study, which found that computer science students outperformed liberal arts students on knowledge tests but displayed equally risky behaviours in practice, particularly regarding social media privacy and password reuse. The implication is that IT education, while it improves awareness, does not automatically translate into consistently safer day-to-day digital behaviour—a finding that challenges curriculum designers to embed practical behavioural change components into IT programmes.

**H5 — Risk Mitigation Practices.** IT students (M = 3.625) adopted significantly more risk mitigation practices than non-IT students (M = 3.265), with a medium effect size (d = 0.641) and p < .001. This is the most consistent and practically meaningful finding of the comparative analysis: IT students are not only more aware of risks but also more likely to take concrete protective action. This result is consistent with Khatiwada (2025) and Adhikari et al. (2026), and supports the view that IT education contributes to a more comprehensive cybersecurity-protective disposition—even if its benefits for day-to-day digital behaviour are more modest. It reinforces the Technology Acceptance Model's prediction that IT students, with greater familiarity with security tools, perceive them as more useful and easier to implement (Davis, 1989), thereby adopting them more readily.

## 5.3 Conclusions

This study set out to assess the influence of cybersecurity awareness on digital behaviour and risk mitigation practices among undergraduate students at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges in Nepal, with a comparative focus on IT and non-IT students. Based on the empirical findings, the following conclusions are drawn:

**Conclusion 1.** Pokhara University students possess a moderate-to-high level of cybersecurity awareness (M = 3.586), with relatively stronger awareness of technical threats and tools compared to legal and procedural knowledge. Awareness deficits are most pronounced in knowledge of Nepal's legal cybersecurity framework and incident reporting procedures.

**Conclusion 2.** Students' digital behaviour is only moderately secure (M = 2.973), with no single behavioural item reaching a mean above 3.06. The knowledge–behaviour gap identified in the international literature is empirically confirmed in the Nepalese context: moderate awareness does not translate consistently into secure daily digital practices.

**Conclusion 3.** Risk mitigation practices are at a moderate level (M = 3.456), with students more consistent in basic technical protections (antivirus, HTTPS checking) than in legal awareness and incident reporting. Legal dimensions of cybersecurity protection are systematically underdeveloped.

**Conclusion 4.** Cybersecurity awareness does not positively predict digital behaviour in the expected direction (H1 not supported). The significant negative relationship found suggests complex self-evaluative dynamics and indicates that awareness-only interventions are insufficient to improve daily secure digital practices.

**Conclusion 5.** Cybersecurity awareness is a strong and direct predictor of risk mitigation practices (H2 supported), explaining 62.9% of variance (β = 0.793, p < .001). Awareness education targeted at Nepalese university students is likely to translate directly into improved adoption of protective tools and behaviours.

**Conclusion 6.** IT students demonstrate significantly higher cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices than non-IT peers (H3, H5 supported, medium effect sizes). The behavioural advantage of IT students over non-IT students is statistically significant but more modest in magnitude (H4 supported, small-medium effect size), confirming that IT education improves awareness and protective action more than day-to-day digital behaviour.

## 5.4 Implications

### 5.4.1 Practical Implications

**For students.** The study finds that cybersecurity awareness is the single strongest predictor of risk mitigation adoption. Students who invest in improving their awareness—through self-directed learning, institutional workshops, or online courses—are substantially more likely to adopt effective protective measures. Students should also prioritise building awareness of Nepal's legal cybersecurity framework, as knowledge of legal remedies and reporting procedures represents a critical but neglected dimension of personal cyber protection (Bhandari, 2025).

**For educational institutions.** Pokhara University-affiliated colleges should treat cybersecurity awareness as a core graduate competency, not an optional or incidental topic. For IT students, programmes should explicitly embed practical behavioural training—not just theoretical knowledge—to bridge the gap between what students know and how they behave daily. For non-IT students, mandatory introductory cybersecurity modules covering threat awareness, basic protective tools, and legal provisions should be incorporated into all undergraduate programmes. The finding that non-IT students are significantly less aware and less protected than IT students underscores the urgency of differentiated, curriculum-embedded interventions.

**For university administrators and policymakers.** The results call for institutionalised cybersecurity awareness campaigns at Pokhara University-affiliated colleges, targeting all student populations but with a particular emphasis on non-IT students who constitute a high-risk, under-served majority. Colleges should establish cybersecurity help desks, incident reporting channels, and peer awareness ambassadors. At the national policy level, the findings support the case for integrating cybersecurity literacy requirements into Nepal's national higher education policy framework, building on the foundation provided by the National Cyber Security Policy 2023 (Gupta, 2024; Adhikari et al., 2025). Regular national cybersecurity awareness campaigns—particularly on legal rights and reporting procedures—are urgently needed.

**For curriculum designers.** Given that awareness strongly predicts risk mitigation but has a complex and counterintuitive relationship with digital behaviour, curriculum design should move beyond information transfer toward experiential and skills-based learning approaches. Simulation exercises (phishing tests, mock incident responses), case studies based on Nepalese cyber incidents, and hands-on tool training are likely to produce stronger and more durable behavioural change than lecture-based awareness programmes alone.

### 5.4.2 Theoretical Implications

**For Protection Motivation Theory (PMT).** The extremely strong predictive relationship between CA and RMP (R² = .629) provides strong empirical support for PMT's core proposition that threat and coping appraisals jointly drive protective motivation (Rogers, 1975, 1983). In the Nepalese university context, the results suggest that awareness operates primarily through the coping appraisal pathway: students who are aware of both threats and effective countermeasures are highly motivated to adopt those countermeasures. Future theoretical development should examine whether the effect of awareness on mitigation is fully mediated by coping appraisal, or whether there are direct effects that bypass appraisal processes.

**For the Information Security Awareness Model (ISAM).** The failure of H1 highlights a theoretical gap in the ISAM's triadic model of knowledge–attitude–behaviour. While knowledge (operationalised as CA) strongly predicts protective action (RMP), it does not straightforwardly predict positive digital behaviour (DB). This suggests that the attitude component plays a crucial mediating role: awareness may improve attitudes toward protective tools more directly than it improves moment-to-moment behavioural habits. Future research should explicitly model attitude as a mediator between awareness and digital behaviour to test this proposition formally.

**For the awareness–behaviour gap literature.** The finding that higher awareness is negatively (not positively) associated with self-reported digital behaviour represents a novel contribution to the awareness–behaviour gap literature. It raises the important methodological question of whether self-report instruments accurately capture digital behaviour, or whether more aware respondents introduce systematic downward bias by applying stricter self-evaluative standards. Researchers should consider supplementing self-report data with behavioural observation or log-based digital behaviour tracking to triangulate findings.

## 5.5 Limitations

This study was conducted with methodological rigor, but several limitations must be acknowledged:

**1. Sampling limitations.** The sample is drawn from Pokhara University-affiliated colleges in Nepal and is not a probability-based random sample. While purposive and convenience sampling are appropriate for the explanatory and comparative goals of this research, the findings may not be fully generalised to all Pokhara University students or to students at other Nepalese universities (Tribhuvan University, Kathmandu University). Future studies employing stratified random sampling would provide stronger grounds for generalisation.

**2. Self-report bias.** All data are collected through self-administered questionnaires measuring self-reported awareness, behaviour, and practices. Social desirability bias may lead to overestimation of awareness and protective practices, while the awareness-based self-evaluation effect discussed above may produce underestimation of digital behaviour quality among more aware students. These biases work in opposite directions, complicating the interpretation of findings.

**3. Digital Behaviour scale reliability.** The low Cronbach's alpha for the Digital Behaviour scale (α = 0.064) is a notable limitation. While multidimensional scales can legitimately produce lower alpha values, the reliability of composite DB scores must be treated with caution. Results from the DB-related analyses (H1, H4) should be interpreted in light of this limitation.

**4. Cross-sectional design.** The cross-sectional snapshot prevents causal inference. While strong regression relationships are reported (particularly for H2), longitudinal designs are necessary to establish that awareness precedes and causes changes in risk mitigation practices over time.

**5. Absence of qualitative depth.** The exclusive use of quantitative methods prevents exploration of the reasons behind observed patterns. Why do IT students, despite superior awareness, not show proportionally superior digital behaviour? What motivates or inhibits specific risk mitigation actions? Mixed-methods designs incorporating student interviews or focus groups would provide contextually rich answers to these questions.

**6. Temporal context.** Findings reflect the cybersecurity threat environment as experienced during the 2024–25 academic year. Rapid evolution in AI-generated phishing, deepfake social engineering, and other emerging threats may alter awareness and behaviour patterns in subsequent cohorts.

## 5.6 Recommendations for Future Research

Building on the findings and limitations of this study, the following directions for future research are proposed:

**1. Longitudinal study design.** A longitudinal study tracking students' cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, and risk mitigation practices from enrolment to graduation would provide causal evidence of how awareness evolves over the course of a degree programme and whether institutional interventions produce lasting behavioural change.

**2. Mixed-methods investigation.** Qualitative follow-up studies—through semi-structured interviews or focus groups with IT and non-IT students—would provide deep contextual insight into the mechanisms driving the awareness–behaviour gap and the differential outcomes observed between student groups.

**3. Multi-university comparative study.** Extending the sampling frame to include Tribhuvan University, Kathmandu University, and private colleges would enable systematic comparison of cybersecurity outcomes across Nepal's major higher education institutions and examine whether institutional factors (programme quality, cybersecurity infrastructure, faculty expertise) moderate individual-level outcomes.

**4. Revised Digital Behaviour measurement.** Future studies should review and revise the Digital Behaviour scale to address the low internal consistency observed in this study. Options include adopting sub-dimension-specific scales, using behavioural simulation tasks, or incorporating digitally logged behaviour data as an objective behavioural measure alongside self-report.

**5. Attitudinal and motivational factors.** Research examining cybersecurity attitude, self-efficacy, and subjective norms as mediators between awareness and behaviour would contribute important theoretical refinements and practical insights into what distinguishes students who translate awareness into action from those who do not.

**6. Intervention effectiveness studies.** Quasi-experimental studies testing the effectiveness of specific cybersecurity training interventions—such as phishing simulation exercises, gamified learning modules, or peer education programmes—in producing measurable improvements in awareness, behaviour, and risk mitigation practices would provide actionable evidence for curriculum designers and institutional administrators.

**7. Nepal-specific scale development.** The validated scales used in this study are primarily adapted from international instruments. Development and validation of a Nepal-specific cybersecurity awareness and behaviour scale—incorporating items relevant to Nepal's digital infrastructure, legal framework, and common threat landscape—would improve measurement validity for future Nepalese research.
