# CHAPTER II: LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

## 2.1 Conceptual Review

### 2.1.1 Cybersecurity Awareness

Cybersecurity awareness refers to the degree to which individuals understand the nature of cyber threats, recognise the importance of protecting digital assets, and possess the knowledge necessary to adopt secure online practices (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). It encompasses not only theoretical knowledge of threats such as phishing, malware, and social engineering, but also the practical capacity to translate that knowledge into protective behaviour (Kovačević et al., 2020). Cybersecurity awareness is therefore regarded as a precondition for secure digital conduct: without it, even technically capable users remain vulnerable to deception and exploitation.

In the context of higher education, awareness has become a matter of institutional urgency. Universities store large volumes of sensitive personal data and cutting-edge research, making them attractive targets for cybercriminals, state-sponsored actors, and hacktivists (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). Yet the free flow of students, staff, and visiting researchers, combined with cultures of open access, creates significant information security challenges. Several scholars have observed that although young people are classified as "digital natives," their technical fluency does not automatically translate into cybersecurity competence (Kovačević et al., 2020; Mai & Tick, 2021). Kovačević et al. (2020) found that university students—despite near-universal internet use—do not feel safe in the cyber environment, do not behave securely, and lack adequate knowledge to protect themselves. Knowledge emerged as the single dominant factor predicting secure behaviour in their study, underscoring the centrality of awareness.

Cybersecurity awareness is typically conceptualised along three interrelated dimensions: **knowledge** (understanding of threats and protective tools), **attitude** (the value placed on security), and **behaviour** (the actions taken to protect oneself online). Anwar et al. (2023) confirmed through Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modelling (PLS-SEM) that all three dimensions—alongside training—have a statistically significant positive impact on cybersecurity awareness among university students. Similarly, Bognar and Bottyán (2024) developed and validated a Personal Cybersecurity Awareness Scale for university students using Exploratory and Confirmatory Factor Analyses (EFA and CFA), identifying five empirically distinct dimensions of cybersecurity behaviour. Their cross-disciplinary analysis demonstrated that awareness varies significantly by academic background, with IT-specialised students displaying stronger awareness than their non-IT peers.

In the Nepalese context, cybersecurity awareness among students and institutions remains critically underdeveloped. Adhikari et al. (2026) assessed cybersecurity literacy proficiency among 221 bachelor's-degree students in Nepal, finding that students have poor awareness of cybersecurity threats, noticeable gaps in cybersecurity education, widespread digital vulnerabilities, and limited impact from current training initiatives. Importantly, their study found no statistically significant association between cybersecurity awareness level and gender—a result that contrasts with findings from some global studies. Similarly, Khatiwada (2025) conducted a quantitative survey among 148 students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus, Hetauda, Nepal, revealing that the majority lacked sufficient awareness of safe practices including password management, device protection, and secure online behaviour; more than one-third had experienced a cyber incident, yet most expressed low confidence in handling such threats. Bhandari (2025) reported that among Nepalese university students, only 46.9% showed awareness of Nepal's cybersecurity legal framework, even though 67.2% were familiar with the concept of "hacking," highlighting a critical disconnect between basic terminology and actionable knowledge.

Taken together, the literature establishes cybersecurity awareness as a multi-dimensional construct—encompassing knowledge, attitude, and behaviour—that is systematically low among university students globally and particularly in Nepal, where structural factors such as weak institutional support, limited curriculum integration, and low digital literacy compound the problem.

### 2.1.2 Digital Behaviour

Digital behaviour refers to the patterns of actions individuals adopt when using digital technologies, including the internet, mobile devices, social media platforms, and cloud-based applications. In the cybersecurity literature, digital behaviour is operationalised through specific security-relevant practices: password management, social media usage patterns, responses to suspicious online content, software update habits, and the extent to which users verify the authenticity of digital communications before acting on them (Mai & Tick, 2021; Jangid, 2025).

The gap between what users know and what they actually do is one of the most persistent findings in the cybersecurity awareness literature. Jangid (2025) found that among college students, 64% used the same password across multiple platforms and 58% had clicked on unknown or suspicious links, despite 90% having heard of phishing. Only 22% enabled two-factor authentication, and only 35% could correctly identify a phishing email. This "awareness–behaviour gap" suggests that knowledge is necessary but not sufficient for secure digital behaviour; attitudinal and contextual factors also play a role. Ahamed et al. (2024) confirmed this in a PLS-SEM study of 430 university students in Bangladesh, finding that cybersecurity attitude mediates the relationship between cybersecurity knowledge, password security, and self-perception of skills on the one hand, and cybersecurity awareness on the other.

Mai and Tick (2021) measured digital behaviour across four dimensions in a comparative study of 313 university students in Hungary and Vietnam: malware-related behaviour, password usage, social engineering susceptibility, and online scam awareness. They found that all respondents, irrespective of nationality, possessed a lack of knowledge of cybersecurity, leading to low levels of cyber threat awareness, with only minor differences in behaviour between the two national groups. Their study underscores that risky digital behaviour is not confined to developing countries but is a global phenomenon, albeit shaped by cultural factors.

Alqahtani (2022) identified password security, browser security, and social media behaviour as the three primary determinants of cybersecurity awareness among university students (N = 450), with password security emerging as the strongest predictor (β = 0.147, p = 0.0001). Elrayah and Jamil (2023), in a study of 235 individuals in Saudi Arabia, further demonstrated that digital literacy—including copyright awareness, responsible citizenship, content curation, and online connectedness—significantly predicted cybersecurity behaviour, with cybersecurity awareness serving as a positive moderator of these relationships.

In Nepal, Dhungana et al. (2023) surveyed 891 students and 157 teachers in K-12 settings, finding that students were increasingly exposed to high-risk cyberspace without adequate preparation. Teachers demonstrated very low cybersecurity competence, thereby failing to model or instill good digital behaviour in students. Adhikari et al. (2025), examining 251 organisations across Nepalese sectors, observed that private organisations demonstrated higher cybersecurity awareness and better digital practices than public institutions, and that small and medium enterprises (SMEs) were particularly vulnerable due to financial and technical constraints.

Digital behaviour is thus shaped by a complex interplay of individual knowledge, attitudinal factors, social norms, and contextual variables including the quality of institutional support, peer influence, and the availability of security tools. In higher education settings, understanding students' digital behaviour patterns is essential for designing interventions that bridge the knowledge–action gap.

### 2.1.3 Risk Mitigation Practices

Risk mitigation practices in cybersecurity refer to the concrete actions individuals take to reduce their exposure to cyber threats and to limit the impact of any security incident that does occur. These practices typically include the installation and use of antivirus software and firewalls, adoption of strong and unique passwords, enabling multi-factor authentication, keeping software up to date, using Virtual Private Networks (VPNs), practising safe browsing (e.g., verifying HTTPS connections and URL authenticity), and protecting personal data through encryption or careful sharing behaviour (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Khatiwada, 2025).

The literature distinguishes between preventive and responsive risk mitigation. Preventive measures are proactive steps taken before a threat materialises—such as using password managers, installing antivirus software, and avoiding suspicious links. Responsive measures are actions taken after an incident, such as changing compromised passwords, reporting an attack, or seeking technical help. Studies consistently find that students are more familiar with the concept of preventive measures than they are diligent in implementing them. Jangid (2025) found that while 70% of students had antivirus software installed, only 30% regularly updated their software and applications, suggesting partial adoption of risk mitigation practices.

Bhandari (2025) noted that Nepalese university students show significant gaps in understanding the legal frameworks available for risk mitigation, including the Electronic Transactions Act and the National Cyber Security Policy 2023. He argued that legal knowledge constitutes an important but underappreciated dimension of risk mitigation, as students who are unaware of their rights and the consequences of cybercrimes are less likely to report incidents or seek redress. His study recommended multi-faceted approaches including legal education, practical cybersecurity training, curriculum design, and institutional policies.

Alqarni (2025), in a large-sample study of 1,980 Saudi secondary school students, found that cybersecurity awareness positively predicted data protection behaviours both directly (β = 0.38, p < 0.001) and indirectly through cyber threat perception (indirect β = 0.17, p < 0.001). Crucially, internet usage duration moderated these effects, with students who spent more time online deriving the greatest benefit from enhanced awareness. This finding suggests that risk mitigation practices are not static: they can be cultivated through targeted awareness programmes, particularly for high-exposure users.

Gupta (2024) observed that in the Nepalese context, inadequate cybersecurity awareness contributes to increased vulnerability to cyber-attacks, data breaches, and societal and economic harm. He proposed AI-driven solutions and argued that strategies emphasising education, training, and regulatory frameworks are vital for addressing these challenges. Gupta (2025) further demonstrated that data-driven and personalised training approaches are more effective than generic interventions in improving cybersecurity awareness and, by extension, risk mitigation practices.

### 2.1.4 Cyber Threats and Online Risks (Phishing, Malware, Social Engineering)

Understanding the specific nature of cyber threats is a prerequisite for effective risk mitigation. The major categories of cyber threats relevant to university students include phishing, malware, social engineering, identity theft, ransomware, and data breaches.

**Phishing** is a social-engineering attack in which an attacker impersonates a legitimate entity—such as a bank, university, or technology company—to deceive the target into revealing sensitive information such as passwords or credit card numbers (Ulven & Wangen, 2021). Phishing remains one of the most prevalent and successful attack vectors because it exploits human rather than technical vulnerabilities. Jangid (2025) found that although 90% of college students had heard of phishing, only 35% could correctly identify a phishing email, and 58% had clicked on unknown or suspicious links. Hasan et al. (2025) reported similar findings in Bangladesh, noting mixed awareness and compliance across student cohorts.

**Malware** is malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorised access to computer systems. Types include viruses, worms, Trojans, spyware, adware, and ransomware. Ransomware—which encrypts a victim's data and demands payment for decryption—has become increasingly prevalent in higher education settings (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Bhandari, 2025). Nepal is no exception: Gupta (2024) documented that ransomware, phishing scams, and data breaches are among the most commonly reported cyber threats in the Nepalese digital environment, exacerbated by low public awareness and weak institutional defences.

**Social engineering** is a broader category of manipulation that exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities to gain unauthorised access to systems or information. It includes pretexting, baiting, quid pro quo attacks, and tailgating, as well as phishing. Mai and Tick (2021) measured social engineering susceptibility as one of four behavioural dimensions in their comparative study, finding it to be an area of particular weakness across both their national samples.

**Identity theft and data breaches** are closely related threats in which personal information is stolen and misused for financial gain, fraud, or reputational damage. University students are particularly at risk because they generate and share large amounts of personal data online through academic platforms, social media, and e-commerce sites (Adhikari et al., 2026; Khatiwada, 2025).

The Nepalese cybersecurity context is characterised by a rapid expansion of internet penetration without a commensurate development of security infrastructure, awareness, or legal enforcement capability. Gupta (2025) observed that Nepal's increasing reliance on digital platforms—e-commerce, e-banking, online education—has heightened the risks posed by these threats, while awareness remains low among both individuals and organisations.

## 2.2 Theoretical Review

### 2.2.1 Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)

The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), originally proposed by Davis (1989), provides a foundational framework for understanding how individuals come to adopt and use information technologies. The model identifies two primary constructs: **Perceived Usefulness (PU)**—the degree to which a user believes a technology will improve their performance—and **Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU)**—the degree to which a user believes the technology will be free from effort. Both constructs influence the user's attitude toward the technology, which in turn determines behavioural intention to use it, ultimately leading to actual system use.

In the cybersecurity context, TAM has been applied to explain why users adopt—or fail to adopt—protective technologies such as antivirus software, password managers, VPNs, and multi-factor authentication systems. If students perceive security tools as difficult to use (low PEOU) or as not meaningfully improving their safety (low PU), they are less likely to adopt them regardless of their awareness of threats. Cheng et al. (2022) applied TAM principles in examining institutional cybersecurity strategies in higher education, finding that faculty and students were more likely to comply with security protocols when they perceived those protocols as both useful and not overly burdensome.

The relevance of TAM to the present study lies in its capacity to explain variations in security tool adoption between IT and non-IT students. IT students may perceive security tools as both more useful and easier to use due to their technical training, thereby exhibiting higher adoption rates. Non-IT students, by contrast, may experience higher perceived complexity, leading to lower adoption—even when threat awareness is similar. TAM thus provides a framework for interpreting the comparative dimension of this research (Hypothesis 5: significant difference in risk mitigation practices between IT and non-IT students).

### 2.2.2 Protection Motivation Theory (PMT)

Protection Motivation Theory (PMT), developed by Rogers (1975) and later extended by Rogers (1983), was originally formulated to explain health-protective behaviour in response to fear appeals. It has since been widely applied in information security research to explain why individuals adopt—or fail to adopt—protective cybersecurity behaviours (Ulven & Wangen, 2021; Alqarni, 2025).

PMT proposes that protective behaviour is motivated by two parallel cognitive appraisal processes:

1. **Threat Appraisal**: An assessment of the severity of a potential threat and the individual's susceptibility to it. High perceived severity and high perceived susceptibility increase motivation to protect oneself.

2. **Coping Appraisal**: An assessment of the effectiveness of available coping responses (response efficacy), the individual's confidence in executing those responses (self-efficacy), and the costs involved (response cost). High response efficacy and self-efficacy, combined with low response cost, increase coping motivation.

When both appraisals are favourable, they converge on **protection motivation**, which drives the intention to adopt protective behaviour. In cybersecurity, this translates into a student who understands that phishing is a severe and realistic threat (threat appraisal), believes that using a spam filter and verifying URLs is effective (response efficacy), and feels capable of implementing those measures (self-efficacy), being more likely to adopt safe browsing practices.

Alqarni (2025) demonstrated that cyber threat perception—a construct closely aligned with PMT's threat appraisal—mediated the relationship between cybersecurity awareness and data protection behaviours (indirect β = 0.17, p < 0.001). This supports the PMT prediction that awareness must be translated into a felt sense of threat before it produces protective action. PMT is therefore directly applicable to understanding why Nepalese university students who are broadly aware of cyber threats may nonetheless fail to implement risk mitigation practices (Adhikari et al., 2026; Khatiwada, 2025).

For the present study, PMT explains the pathway from cybersecurity awareness (Hypothesis 2) to risk mitigation practices: awareness of threats must engage both threat appraisal and coping appraisal to produce protective behaviour. Interventions that raise awareness without simultaneously building self-efficacy and demonstrating the effectiveness of mitigation tools may fail to change behaviour.

### 2.2.3 Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB)

The Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB), formulated by Ajzen (1991), proposes that human behaviour is best predicted by **behavioural intention**, which is itself determined by three antecedents:

1. **Attitude toward the behaviour**: The individual's overall positive or negative evaluation of performing the behaviour.
2. **Subjective norms**: The perceived social pressure from important referent groups to perform or not perform the behaviour.
3. **Perceived behavioural control (PBC)**: The perceived ease or difficulty of performing the behaviour, reflecting both self-efficacy and controllability.

In cybersecurity research, TPB has been used to predict security-compliant behaviour such as password management, software updating, and phishing avoidance. Ahamed et al. (2024) found that cybersecurity attitude—an evaluative construct central to TPB—served as a significant mediator between knowledge and password security on the one hand and cybersecurity awareness on the other. This suggests that attitude shapes how awareness is enacted in behaviour, consistent with TPB's logic.

Subjective norms are particularly relevant in the university context, where peer culture can either reinforce or undermine security behaviour. Students who observe peers being casual about password security or mocking security protocols may internalise lower subjective norms for secure behaviour. Conversely, universities that create a culture of security consciousness may elevate subjective norms and thereby improve compliance (Bognar & Bottyán, 2024).

Perceived behavioural control explains why even students with positive attitudes toward cybersecurity may fail to act: if they believe they lack the technical skills to implement security measures—a perception more common among non-IT students—they will have lower behavioural intentions and correspondingly weaker risk mitigation practices. TPB thus provides a complementary explanation to TAM for the anticipated differences between IT and non-IT students across all three dimensions of the present study.

### 2.2.4 Information Security Awareness Model (ISAM)

The Information Security Awareness Model (ISAM), building on foundational work by Siponen (2000) and Kruger and Kearney (2006), conceptualises information security awareness as a triadic construct comprising **knowledge** (what people know about security), **attitude** (how they feel about security), and **behaviour** (what they actually do). The model posits that effective security awareness requires alignment across all three components: knowledge without a positive attitude will not reliably produce secure behaviour, and positive attitudes without adequate knowledge leave gaps that attackers can exploit.

Anwar et al. (2023) operationalised this triadic structure in their PLS-SEM study of university students, confirming that knowledge, attitude, behaviour, and training each contribute positively and significantly to overall cybersecurity awareness. Their study reinforces the ISAM's theoretical architecture and provides empirical support for its application in higher education contexts.

The ISAM is particularly useful for interpreting the Nepalese findings. Adhikari et al. (2026) found that Nepalese bachelor's students were familiar with common cyberattacks (knowledge) but demonstrated poor practical awareness (behaviour gap), suggesting that knowledge alone is insufficient without attitudinal reinforcement and practical training. Bhandari (2025) similarly identified a disconnect between familiarity with hacking terminology (surface-level knowledge) and awareness of legal frameworks and risk mitigation practices (deeper, action-relevant knowledge), consistent with the ISAM prediction that shallow knowledge does not produce protective behaviour.

For the present study, the ISAM provides the conceptual architecture for operationalising cybersecurity awareness as an independent variable with knowledge, attitude, and behavioural components, each of which can be measured through survey items and compared between IT and non-IT students.

## 2.3 Empirical Review

### 2.3.1 Relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Digital Behaviour

The relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behaviour is one of the most extensively studied questions in the information security literature, and the findings are remarkably consistent: awareness is a necessary but not sufficient condition for secure digital behaviour (Kovačević et al., 2020; Anwar et al., 2023; Elrayah & Jamil, 2023).

Elrayah and Jamil (2023) examined this relationship among 235 individuals in Saudi Arabia, finding that digital literacy—an enabler and correlate of cybersecurity awareness—significantly predicted cybersecurity behaviour across all four of their digital literacy dimensions (copyright, citizenship, curation, and connectedness). Critically, cybersecurity awareness moderated these relationships, amplifying the positive effects of digital literacy on behaviour. This finding suggests that awareness does not merely add to digital literacy's effect on behaviour; it multiplies it—individuals with high awareness translate their digital literacy into far better security behaviour than those with low awareness.

Anwar et al. (2023) confirmed a positive and significant relationship between knowledge, attitude, and cybersecurity awareness, which in turn influenced behaviour, using PLS-SEM among Indonesian university students. The study supports Hypothesis 1 of the present research—that there is a positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and digital behaviour.

Mai and Tick (2021) found that in both Hungary and Vietnam, low cybersecurity awareness correlated with poor security behaviour across all four measured dimensions (malware, password, social engineering, online scam), despite the absence of major cultural differences between the two samples. The consistency of this pattern across different national contexts strengthens the case for a robust causal link between awareness and behaviour.

Ahamed et al. (2024) further refined this relationship by demonstrating that cybersecurity attitude mediates between knowledge and behaviour: students who had strong cybersecurity knowledge but a neutral or negative attitude toward security practices were less likely to translate that knowledge into secure digital behaviour. This has implications for educational interventions in Nepal, where knowledge levels are low and attitudes may be compounded by a perception that cybersecurity is primarily a concern for IT professionals.

Bognar and Bottyán (2024), using structural equation modelling, found that students in technical disciplines exhibited systematically stronger cybersecurity behaviour than those in humanities and social sciences, even after controlling for general technology usage patterns. This supports the hypothesis that IT students in the present study will demonstrate better digital behaviour than non-IT peers (Hypothesis 4).

### 2.3.2 Relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Risk Mitigation Practices

The literature provides strong support for a positive relationship between cybersecurity awareness and the adoption of risk mitigation practices (Hypothesis 2).

Alqarni (2025), in the largest-sample study reviewed (N = 1,980), found that cybersecurity awareness was a significant and positive predictor of data protection behaviours (β = 0.38, p < 0.001), and that cyber threat perception mediated this relationship (indirect β = 0.17, p < 0.001). The moderation by internet usage duration further suggested that the effect is strongest among high-frequency internet users—a characteristic that describes most university students in Nepal.

Khatiwada (2025) found that students at Makawanpur Multiple Campus who reported higher cybersecurity awareness also reported more consistent adoption of protective practices such as password management and device protection. However, the gap between stated awareness and actual practice remained substantial, corroborating the awareness–behaviour gap identified in the broader literature.

Bhandari (2025) argued that legal knowledge constitutes a neglected dimension of risk mitigation: students who understand Nepal's Electronic Transactions Act, the National Cyber Security Policy 2023, and the remedies available to cyber-crime victims are better positioned to seek help, report incidents, and take preventive measures. The finding that only 46.9% of Nepalese university students were aware of the country's cybersecurity legal framework implies that interventions focused solely on technical awareness may miss an important dimension of risk mitigation.

Nithyasri and Nithya Sumathi (2022), examining 300 college students in Coimbatore, India, using factor analysis and confirmatory factor analysis, identified three underlying factors of cyber security behaviour: cyber knowledge, security implications, and cyber threats. Students with higher scores on cyber knowledge were significantly more likely to adopt security implications (risk mitigation practices), providing further empirical support for the awareness–mitigation link.

### 2.3.3 Relationship between Digital Behaviour and Risk Mitigation

The relationship between digital behaviour and risk mitigation practices is bidirectional and mutually reinforcing, but the dominant causal direction in the literature runs from safer digital behaviour to stronger risk mitigation (Jangid, 2025; Alqahtani, 2022).

Alqahtani (2022) found that students with stronger password security practices were significantly more likely to also adopt other risk mitigation measures, suggesting that password behaviour serves as a gateway to a broader security posture. Students who use strong, unique passwords tend also to be more likely to install and update antivirus software, avoid suspicious links, and verify website authenticity before entering personal information. This clustering of protective practices around a common security orientation implies that digital behaviour and risk mitigation practices are facets of the same underlying security disposition.

Hasan et al. (2025) found in Bangladesh that compliance with secure digital behaviour—including avoiding unknown downloads and verifying email senders—was associated with higher adoption of protective tools such as antivirus software and browser extensions. Conversely, students with poor digital behaviour—such as regularly clicking on unverified links—were also less likely to take proactive mitigation steps, even when they reported knowing that such steps were important.

Jangid (2025) observed that the minority of students who consistently practised safe digital behaviour (checking URLs, enabling 2FA, using separate passwords) also reported the highest adoption of risk mitigation tools, including regularly updated antivirus software and encrypted communication applications. This pattern underscores the complementarity of digital behaviour and risk mitigation as dimensions of a secure cybersecurity posture.

### 2.3.4 Mediating Role of Digital Behaviour

A growing body of evidence supports the role of digital behaviour as a mediator between cybersecurity awareness and risk mitigation practices—the pathway hypothesised in the theoretical framework of the present study.

Ahamed et al. (2024) demonstrated that cybersecurity attitude—closely analogous to digital behaviour orientation—mediated the relationship between knowledge and awareness management outcomes. While their study did not explicitly model risk mitigation as the dependent variable, the structural logic is analogous: awareness influences attitudes and behaviour patterns, which in turn shape protective action.

Elrayah and Jamil (2023) found that cybersecurity awareness moderated the effect of digital literacy on cybersecurity behaviour, implying that awareness does not act solely as an independent predictor of risk mitigation but also shapes the pathways through which other factors (including digital behaviour practices) affect protective outcomes.

Alqarni (2025) demonstrated that cyber threat perception—a cognitive-behavioural construct that is shaped by both awareness and prior digital experience—mediated the relationship between awareness and data protection behaviours. This is consistent with the PMT-based argument that awareness must engage threat appraisal and coping appraisal (both of which involve behavioural orientations) before it produces protective action.

In the Nepalese context, Adhikari et al. (2026) and Khatiwada (2025) both implicitly support a mediated model: students who reported more secure digital behaviours (regular password changes, avoidance of suspicious links, careful data sharing) also reported higher adoption of mitigation practices, suggesting that behaviour is a conduit through which awareness influences mitigation. The present study will test this mediation formally, filling a gap in the existing Nepalese literature.

## 2.4 Research Gap

The review of existing literature reveals several significant gaps that the present study is positioned to address.

**First**, while there is a growing body of research on cybersecurity awareness among university students in various regions—including South Asia, the Middle East, Eastern Europe, and Southeast Asia—empirical research specifically focused on University-affiliated students in Nepal is extremely limited. Existing Nepalese studies are either restricted to a single campus (Khatiwada, 2025; Bhandari, 2025) or directed at organisational rather than student populations (Adhikari et al., 2025). Adhikari et al. (2026) provide the most proximate study to the present research, but their focus on Oxford College in Gaidakot does not capture the diversity of University's affiliated colleges in the Gandaki Province, where IT and non-IT programmes coexist in varied institutional contexts.

**Second**, the comparative dimension—examining differences between IT and non-IT students across all three variables (cybersecurity awareness, digital behaviour, and risk mitigation practices)—is underexplored in the Nepalese context. Bognar and Bottyán (2024) performed such a comparison in Hungary and found significant disciplinary differences, and Mai and Tick (2021) compared national cultures, but no published study has examined IT versus non-IT differences within a Nepalese university population. This gap is particularly consequential given that non-IT students constitute the majority of the student body and represent a high-risk, under-served group.

**Third**, the mediating role of digital behaviour in the awareness–mitigation relationship has not been formally tested in the Nepalese context. While several global studies provide indirect support for this mediation (Ahamed et al., 2024; Alqarni, 2025), no study in Nepal has explicitly modelled digital behaviour as a mediating variable. The present study will provide the first empirical test of this mediated pathway for Nepali university students.

**Fourth**, Ulven and Wangen (2021) noted that empirical research on cybersecurity risks in higher education is scarce and that there are large gaps in the literature, particularly regarding the student perspective in developing countries. Nepal's rapidly expanding digital infrastructure, combined with its nascent cybersecurity policy environment (National Cyber Security Policy 2023), makes this an urgent and timely area of investigation.

## 2.5 Theoretical Framework

The theoretical framework of the present study integrates the conceptual and theoretical foundations reviewed above into a structural model with three core variables.

**Independent Variable: Cybersecurity Awareness**
Cybersecurity awareness is operationalised through three sub-dimensions—knowledge of threats (familiarity with phishing, malware, social engineering, and data breaches), awareness of security tools (knowledge of antivirus software, VPNs, firewalls, and multi-factor authentication), and safe internet practices (understanding of password security, secure browsing, and data protection norms). This operationalisation draws on the Information Security Awareness Model (Siponen, 2000; Kruger & Kearney, 2006) and is consistent with the measurement approaches used by Anwar et al. (2023), Bognar and Bottyán (2024), and Adhikari et al. (2026).

**Mediating Variable: Digital Behaviour**
Digital behaviour captures the security-relevant patterns through which cybersecurity awareness is enacted in everyday digital life. It is operationalised through three sub-dimensions: password practices (use of strong/unique passwords, two-factor authentication, and password manager tools), social media usage (privacy settings, information-sharing behaviour, and response to social engineering attempts), and online decision-making (link verification, recognition of phishing attempts, and reactions to unsolicited communications). The mediation hypothesis draws on PMT (Rogers, 1975, 1983), TPB (Ajzen, 1991), and the empirical work of Ahamed et al. (2024) and Alqarni (2025).

**Dependent Variable: Risk Mitigation Practices**
Risk mitigation practices capture the concrete protective actions students take to reduce their cyber-risk exposure. It is operationalised through three sub-dimensions: use of antivirus/firewall software, safe browsing habits (URL verification, HTTPS usage, avoidance of suspicious downloads), and data protection practices (careful data sharing, backup behaviour, and awareness of legal remedies). This operationalisation is consistent with Khatiwada (2025), Bhandari (2025), and Ulven and Wangen (2021).

**Structural Relationships:**

The framework proposes:

- A direct positive effect of Cybersecurity Awareness on Digital Behaviour (H1)
- A direct positive effect of Cybersecurity Awareness on Risk Mitigation Practices (H2)
- A mediating effect of Digital Behaviour on the relationship between Cybersecurity Awareness and Risk Mitigation Practices (informed by H1 and H2)
- Significant differences in all three variables between IT and non-IT students (H3, H4, H5)

## 2.6 Operational Definitions

### 2.6.1 Cybersecurity Awareness

For the purposes of this study, **cybersecurity awareness** is defined as the degree to which a student at a University-affiliated college demonstrates: (i) knowledge of the nature, types, and mechanisms of common cyber threats; (ii) familiarity with security tools and their proper use; and (iii) understanding of the safe internet practices that reduce exposure to those threats. Operationally, this construct is measured through a Likert-scale survey instrument assessing three sub-dimensions:

- **Knowledge of threats**: The respondent's ability to identify, describe, and distinguish between phishing, malware, ransomware, social engineering, and data breaches.
- **Awareness of security tools**: Familiarity with antivirus software, firewalls, VPNs, two-factor authentication, password managers, and encrypted communications applications.
- **Safe internet practices**: Understanding of behaviours such as verifying website authenticity, using secure (HTTPS) connections, avoiding unknown downloads, and practising regular software updates.

### 2.6.2 Digital Behaviour

**Digital behaviour** is defined as the security-relevant patterns of action that students consistently exhibit when using digital technologies in their daily academic and personal lives. It is operationalised across three sub-dimensions:

- **Password practices**: The frequency with which respondents use strong, unique passwords across different platforms; enable two-factor authentication; and change passwords following a suspected breach.
- **Social media usage**: The extent to which respondents adjust privacy settings, limit the personal information they share publicly, verify the identity of online contacts, and recognise social engineering attempts on social platforms.
- **Online decision-making**: The degree to which respondents verify URLs and sender identities before clicking links or opening attachments, avoid suspicious or unsolicited downloads, and check website security indicators before entering personal or financial information.

### 2.6.3 Risk Mitigation Practices

**Risk mitigation practices** are defined as the deliberate, proactive steps students take to reduce the likelihood and impact of cybersecurity incidents. This construct is operationalised through three sub-dimensions:

- **Use of antivirus/firewalls**: The installation, activation, and regular updating of antivirus software, device firewalls, and related protective technologies on personal devices.
- **Safe browsing habits**: The consistent adoption of behaviours such as checking for HTTPS in URLs, using secure search engines, clearing browser history and cookies, using ad-blockers, and avoiding public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions.
- **Data protection practices**: Practices for securing personal and academic data, including regular backups, careful sharing of personally identifiable information, use of cloud storage with strong encryption, and awareness of Nepal's Electronic Transactions Act and National Cyber Security Policy 2023 as mechanisms for legal redress.
